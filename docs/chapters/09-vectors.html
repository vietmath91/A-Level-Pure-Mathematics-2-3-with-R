10.1 Overview

This chapter develops six connected vector ideas:

  1. position vectors and displacement vectors;
  2. magnitude, unit vectors and parallel vectors;
  3. vector equations of straight lines;
  4. the scalar product and angles;
  5. intersections of straight lines;
  6. closest points and distances from points to lines.

The central aim is to understand the meaning of a vector equation of a line and to use scalar products for problem solving. We do not need equations of planes in this chapter.

Silver develops simple application.
Gold develops exam-style reasoning.

10.2 9.1 Position vectors and displacement vectors

10.2.1 Key mathematical ideas

A position vector gives the position of a point relative to the origin.

If

\[ \overrightarrow{OA}=\mathbf{a} \]

and

\[ \overrightarrow{OB}=\mathbf{b}, \]

then

\[ \overrightarrow{AB}=\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}. \]

This is one of the most important ideas in the chapter: to move from \(A\) to \(B\), subtract the position vector of \(A\) from the position vector of \(B\).

For a vector

\[ \mathbf{a}=a_1\mathbf{i}+a_2\mathbf{j}+a_3\mathbf{k}, \]

its magnitude is

\[ |\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2}. \]

A unit vector in the direction of a non-zero vector \(\mathbf{a}\) is

\[ \dfrac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}. \]

10.2.3 Worked example

Let

\[ A(2,-1,3) \]

and

\[ B(-1,5,4). \]

Find \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(|\overrightarrow{AB}|\).

The position vectors are

\[ \overrightarrow{OA}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix} \]

and

\[ \overrightarrow{OB}=\begin{pmatrix}-1\\5\\4\end{pmatrix}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{OB}-\overrightarrow{OA} = \begin{pmatrix}-1\\5\\4\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-3\\6\\1\end{pmatrix}. \]

So

\[ |\overrightarrow{AB}| = \sqrt{(-3)^2+6^2+1^2} = \sqrt{46}. \]

10.3 9.2 Vector equations of straight lines

10.3.1 Key mathematical ideas

A straight line is determined by:

  • one point on the line;
  • one direction vector.

If a line passes through the point with position vector \(\mathbf{a}\) and has direction vector \(\mathbf{d}\), then its vector equation is

\[ \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d}, \]

where \(t\) is a real parameter.

Each value of \(t\) gives a point on the line.

If the line passes through points with position vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\), then a direction vector is

\[ \mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}. \]

So the line can be written as

\[ \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+t(\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}). \]

The direction vector is not unique. Any non-zero scalar multiple of a direction vector describes the same direction.

10.3.3 Worked example

Find the vector equation of the line through

\[ A(1,2,-1) \]

and

\[ B(5,-2,3). \]

A direction vector is

\[ \overrightarrow{AB} = \begin{pmatrix}5\\-2\\3\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\-1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4\\-4\\4\end{pmatrix}. \]

We may divide this direction vector by \(4\) and use

\[ \begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\\1\end{pmatrix}. \]

Therefore, one vector equation of the line is

\[ \mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\-1\end{pmatrix} +t\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\\1\end{pmatrix}. \]

10.4 9.3 The scalar product and angles

10.4.1 Key mathematical ideas

The scalar product, or dot product, of two vectors is defined by

\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=|\mathbf{a}|\,|\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta, \]

where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two vectors.

If

\[ \mathbf{a}=a_1\mathbf{i}+a_2\mathbf{j}+a_3\mathbf{k} \]

and

\[ \mathbf{b}=b_1\mathbf{i}+b_2\mathbf{j}+b_3\mathbf{k}, \]

then

\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3. \]

Combining the two formulae gives

\[ \cos\theta= \dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|\,|\mathbf{b}|}. \]

Two non-zero vectors are perpendicular when

\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0. \]

10.4.3 Worked example

Find the angle between

\[ \mathbf{a}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix} \]

and

\[ \mathbf{b}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\2\end{pmatrix}. \]

First compute the scalar product:

\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=2(1)+(-1)(2)+2(2)=4. \]

Also,

\[ |\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{2^2+(-1)^2+2^2}=3 \]

and

\[ |\mathbf{b}|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+2^2}=3. \]

Therefore,

\[ \cos\theta=\dfrac{4}{3\cdot3}=\dfrac{4}{9}. \]

So

\[ \theta=\cos^{-1}\left(\dfrac{4}{9}\right) \approx63.6^\circ. \]

10.5 9.4 Angles between straight lines

10.5.1 Key mathematical ideas

The angle between two straight lines is the angle between their direction vectors.

If the lines have direction vectors \(\mathbf{d}_1\) and \(\mathbf{d}_2\), then

\[ \cos\theta= \dfrac{\mathbf{d}_1\cdot\mathbf{d}_2}{|\mathbf{d}_1|\,|\mathbf{d}_2|}. \]

If the question asks for the acute angle between two lines, use

\[ \cos\theta= \dfrac{|\mathbf{d}_1\cdot\mathbf{d}_2|}{|\mathbf{d}_1|\,|\mathbf{d}_2|}. \]

Two lines are parallel if their direction vectors are scalar multiples.

Two lines are perpendicular if their direction vectors have scalar product zero.

10.5.3 Worked example

Find the acute angle between the lines with direction vectors

\[ \mathbf{d}_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\-2\end{pmatrix} \]

and

\[ \mathbf{d}_2=\begin{pmatrix}3\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}. \]

The scalar product is

\[ \mathbf{d}_1\cdot\mathbf{d}_2=1(3)+2(-1)+(-2)(2)=-3. \]

The magnitudes are

\[ |\mathbf{d}_1|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+(-2)^2}=3 \]

and

\[ |\mathbf{d}_2|=\sqrt{3^2+(-1)^2+2^2}=\sqrt{14}. \]

For the acute angle,

\[ \cos\theta= \dfrac{|-3|}{3\sqrt{14}}= \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{14}}. \]

Hence

\[ \theta\approx74.5^\circ. \]

10.6 9.5 Intersections of straight lines

10.6.1 Key mathematical ideas

To find the point of intersection of two vector lines, write out the parametric equations and solve them simultaneously.

For example, if

\[ \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{d} \]

and

\[ \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{b}+\mu\mathbf{e}, \]

then an intersection point must satisfy

\[ \mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{d}=\mathbf{b}+\mu\mathbf{e}. \]

This gives simultaneous equations in \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\).

In three dimensions, two lines may be:

  • intersecting;
  • parallel;
  • the same line;
  • skew.

Skew lines are not parallel but do not intersect.

10.6.3 Worked example

Find the intersection point of

\[ \mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix} +\lambda\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix} \]

and

\[ \mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\6\end{pmatrix} +\mu\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}. \]

Equating components gives

\[ 1+2\lambda=5-\mu, \]

\[ 2-\lambda=\mu, \]

and

\[ 3\lambda=6. \]

From the third equation,

\[ \lambda=2. \]

Then the second equation gives

\[ \mu=2-2=0. \]

The first equation is also satisfied:

\[ 1+2(2)=5-0. \]

Therefore, the lines intersect at

\[ \begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\6\end{pmatrix}. \]

10.7 9.6 Closest points and distances from points to lines

10.7.1 Key mathematical ideas

The scalar product is very useful for finding the closest point from a point to a line.

Let the line be

\[ \mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d}, \]

and let \(P\) have position vector \(\mathbf{p}\).

A general point on the line is

\[ \mathbf{x}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d}. \]

At the closest point, the vector from the point on the line to \(P\) is perpendicular to the direction vector of the line. Therefore,

\[ (\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{x})\cdot\mathbf{d}=0. \]

Substituting \(\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d}\) gives

\[ (\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a}-t\mathbf{d})\cdot\mathbf{d}=0. \]

So

\[ t=\dfrac{(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{d}}. \]

10.7.3 Worked example

Find the closest point from

\[ P(4,0,5) \]

to the line

\[ \mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix}+t\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}. \]

Here,

\[ \mathbf{a}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{d}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{p}=\begin{pmatrix}4\\0\\5\end{pmatrix}. \]

Using

\[ t=\dfrac{(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{d}}, \]

we get

\[ \mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a}=\begin{pmatrix}3\\-2\\5\end{pmatrix}. \]

So

\[ (\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{d}=3(2)+(-2)(-1)+5(2)=18, \]

and

\[ \mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{d}=2^2+(-1)^2+2^2=9. \]

Hence

\[ t=\dfrac{18}{9}=2. \]

The closest point is

\[ \mathbf{x}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix}+2\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\4\end{pmatrix}. \]

The distance from \(P\) to the line is

\[ \left|\begin{pmatrix}4\\0\\5\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\4\end{pmatrix}\right| = \left|\begin{pmatrix}-1\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}\right| = \sqrt2. \]

  2. The scalar product is \(-3\), and the magnitudes are \(3\) and \(\sqrt{14}\). The acute angle satisfies \[ \cos\theta=\dfrac{3}{3\sqrt{14}}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{14}}. \] Therefore \[ \theta=\cos^{-1}\left(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{14}}\right)\approx74.5^\circ. \]

  3. Equating components gives \(\lambda=2\) and \(\mu=0\), so the intersection point is \((5,0,6)\). The direction vectors are \(\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix}\) and \(\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}\). Hence \[ \cos\theta=\dfrac{|2(-1)+(-1)(1)+3(0)|}{\sqrt{14}\sqrt2}=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{28}}. \] Therefore \(\theta\approx55.5^\circ\).

  4. At the closest point, the vector from the line to the point is perpendicular to the direction vector. Therefore \[ (\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a}-t\mathbf{d})\cdot\mathbf{d}=0. \] Solving gives \[ t=\dfrac{(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{d}}. \] For the given point and line, \(t=2\), so the closest point is \((5,0,4)\).

  5. Equating components gives \[ s=1+2t, \] \[ 2s=2+5t, \] and \[ k+s=3-t. \] From the first two equations, \(t=0\) and \(s=1\). Substituting into the third equation gives \(k+1=3\), so \(k=2\). The point of intersection is \((1,2,3)\).

## 9.1 Position vectors and displacement vectors

### Key mathematical ideas

A **position vector** gives the position of a point relative to the origin.

If

$$
\overrightarrow{OA}=\mathbf{a}
$$

and

$$
\overrightarrow{OB}=\mathbf{b},
$$

then

$$
\overrightarrow{AB}=\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}.
$$

This is one of the most important ideas in the chapter: to move from $A$ to $B$, subtract the position vector of $A$ from the position vector of $B$.

For a vector

$$
\mathbf{a}=a_1\mathbf{i}+a_2\mathbf{j}+a_3\mathbf{k},
$$

its magnitude is

$$
|\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2}.
$$

A unit vector in the direction of a non-zero vector $\mathbf{a}$ is

$$
\dfrac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}.
$$

## 9.2 Vector equations of straight lines

### Key mathematical ideas

A straight line is determined by:

- one point on the line;
- one direction vector.

If a line passes through the point with position vector $\mathbf{a}$ and has direction vector $\mathbf{d}$, then its vector equation is

$$
\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d},
$$

where $t$ is a real parameter.

Each value of $t$ gives a point on the line.

If the line passes through points with position vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$, then a direction vector is

$$
\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}.
$$

So the line can be written as

$$
\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+t(\mathbf{b}-\mathbf{a}).
$$

The direction vector is not unique. Any non-zero scalar multiple of a direction vector describes the same direction.

## 9.3 The scalar product and angles

### Key mathematical ideas

The scalar product, or dot product, of two vectors is defined by

$$
\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=|\mathbf{a}|\,|\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta,
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors.

If

$$
\mathbf{a}=a_1\mathbf{i}+a_2\mathbf{j}+a_3\mathbf{k}
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{b}=b_1\mathbf{i}+b_2\mathbf{j}+b_3\mathbf{k},
$$

then

$$
\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3.
$$

Combining the two formulae gives

$$
\cos\theta=
\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|\,|\mathbf{b}|}.
$$

Two non-zero vectors are perpendicular when

$$
\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0.
$$

### Worked example

Find the angle between

$$
\mathbf{a}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{b}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\2\end{pmatrix}.
$$

First compute the scalar product:

$$
\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=2(1)+(-1)(2)+2(2)=4.
$$

Also,

$$
|\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{2^2+(-1)^2+2^2}=3
$$

and

$$
|\mathbf{b}|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+2^2}=3.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\cos\theta=\dfrac{4}{3\cdot3}=\dfrac{4}{9}.
$$

So

$$
\theta=\cos^{-1}\left(\dfrac{4}{9}\right)
\approx63.6^\circ.
$$

## 9.4 Angles between straight lines

### Key mathematical ideas

The angle between two straight lines is the angle between their direction vectors.

If the lines have direction vectors $\mathbf{d}_1$ and $\mathbf{d}_2$, then

$$
\cos\theta=
\dfrac{\mathbf{d}_1\cdot\mathbf{d}_2}{|\mathbf{d}_1|\,|\mathbf{d}_2|}.
$$

If the question asks for the acute angle between two lines, use

$$
\cos\theta=
\dfrac{|\mathbf{d}_1\cdot\mathbf{d}_2|}{|\mathbf{d}_1|\,|\mathbf{d}_2|}.
$$

Two lines are parallel if their direction vectors are scalar multiples.

Two lines are perpendicular if their direction vectors have scalar product zero.

$$

The scalar product is

## 9.5 Intersections of straight lines

### Key mathematical ideas

To find the point of intersection of two vector lines, write out the parametric equations and solve them simultaneously.

For example, if

$$
\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{d}
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{b}+\mu\mathbf{e},
$$

then an intersection point must satisfy

$$
\mathbf{a}+\lambda\mathbf{d}=\mathbf{b}+\mu\mathbf{e}.
$$

This gives simultaneous equations in $\lambda$ and $\mu$.

In three dimensions, two lines may be:

- intersecting;
- parallel;
- the same line;
- skew.

Skew lines are not parallel but do not intersect.

### Worked example

Find the intersection point of

$$
\mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix}
+\lambda\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\3\end{pmatrix}
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\6\end{pmatrix}
+\mu\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Equating components gives

$$
1+2\lambda=5-\mu,
$$

$$
2-\lambda=\mu,
$$

and

$$
3\lambda=6.
$$

From the third equation,

$$
\lambda=2.
$$

Then the second equation gives

$$
\mu=2-2=0.
$$

The first equation is also satisfied:

$$
1+2(2)=5-0.
$$

Therefore, the lines intersect at

$$
\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\6\end{pmatrix}.
$$

## 9.6 Closest points and distances from points to lines

### Key mathematical ideas

The scalar product is very useful for finding the closest point from a point to a line.

Let the line be

$$
\mathbf{r}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d},
$$

and let $P$ have position vector $\mathbf{p}$.

A general point on the line is

$$
\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d}.
$$

At the closest point, the vector from the point on the line to $P$ is perpendicular to the direction vector of the line. Therefore,

$$
(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{x})\cdot\mathbf{d}=0.
$$

Substituting $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{a}+t\mathbf{d}$ gives

$$
(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a}-t\mathbf{d})\cdot\mathbf{d}=0.
$$

So

$$
t=\dfrac{(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{d}}.
$$

### Worked example

Find the closest point from

$$
P(4,0,5)
$$

to the line

$$
\mathbf{r}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix}+t\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Here,

$$
\mathbf{a}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix},
\quad
\mathbf{d}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix},
\quad
\mathbf{p}=\begin{pmatrix}4\\0\\5\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Using

$$
t=\dfrac{(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{d}}{\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{d}},
$$

we get

$$
\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a}=\begin{pmatrix}3\\-2\\5\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So

$$
(\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{d}=3(2)+(-2)(-1)+5(2)=18,
$$

and

$$
\mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{d}=2^2+(-1)^2+2^2=9.
$$

Hence

$$
t=\dfrac{18}{9}=2.
$$

The closest point is

$$
\mathbf{x}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{pmatrix}+2\begin{pmatrix}2\\-1\\2\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\4\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The distance from $P$ to the line is

$$
\left|\begin{pmatrix}4\\0\\5\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}5\\0\\4\end{pmatrix}\right|
=
\left|\begin{pmatrix}-1\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}\right|
=
\sqrt2.
$$
