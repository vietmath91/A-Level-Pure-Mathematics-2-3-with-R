12.1 Overview

This chapter develops six connected ideas:

  1. imaginary numbers and Cartesian form;
  2. arithmetic, conjugates, modulus and argument;
  3. polar form and geometric transformations;
  4. equations with complex roots;
  5. loci and shaded regions in the complex plane;
  6. mixed problem solving with exact reasoning.

The central aim is to make complex numbers both algebraic and geometric. A complex number

\[ z=x+iy \]

is also the point

\[ (x,y) \]

on an Argand diagram. This link between algebra and geometry is especially important when solving loci questions.

Note

The exercises are organised into tiers.

Bronze develops fluency.
Silver develops application.
Gold develops exam-style reasoning.
The STEP-style section extends the chapter beyond routine A Level fluency.

12.2 12.1 Imaginary numbers and Cartesian form

12.2.1 Key mathematical ideas

The imaginary unit \(i\) is defined by

\[ i^2=-1. \]

A complex number has Cartesian form

\[ z=x+iy, \]

where \(x\) and \(y\) are real numbers.

The real part is

\[ \operatorname{Re}(z)=x, \]

and the imaginary part is

\[ \operatorname{Im}(z)=y. \]

Two complex numbers are equal if and only if their real and imaginary parts are equal. Therefore, if

\[ a+bi=c+di, \]

then

\[ a=c \quad\text{and}\quad b=d. \]

12.2.3 Worked example

Given

\[ 3+2i+a+bi=7-i, \]

find \(a\) and \(b\).

The left-hand side is

\[ (3+a)+(2+b)i. \]

Comparing real and imaginary parts gives

\[ 3+a=7 \]

and

\[ 2+b=-1. \]

Therefore,

\[ a=4 \quad\text{and}\quad b=-3. \]

12.3 12.2 Conjugates, modulus, argument and division

12.3.1 Key mathematical ideas

If

\[ z=x+iy, \]

then the conjugate of \(z\) is

\[ \bar z=x-iy. \]

The modulus of \(z\) is

\[ |z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}. \]

The argument of \(z\) is the angle measured from the positive real axis to the line from the origin to the point representing \(z\).

The key identity is

\[ z\bar z=(x+iy)(x-iy)=x^2+y^2=|z|^2. \]

This is why conjugates are useful for division.

12.3.3 Worked example

Calculate

\[ \dfrac{5+2i}{1-3i}. \]

Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator:

\[ \dfrac{5+2i}{1-3i} = \dfrac{(5+2i)(1+3i)}{(1-3i)(1+3i)}. \]

The numerator is

\[ (5+2i)(1+3i)=5+15i+2i+6i^2=-1+17i. \]

The denominator is

\[ 1^2+3^2=10. \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{5+2i}{1-3i} = \dfrac{-1+17i}{10} = -\dfrac{1}{10}+\dfrac{17}{10}i. \]

12.4 12.3 Polar form, multiplication and transformations

12.4.1 Key mathematical ideas

A complex number can be written in Cartesian form as

\[ z=x+iy. \]

It can also be written in polar form as

\[ z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta), \]

where

\[ r=|z| \]

and

\[ \theta=\arg z. \]

If

\[ z_1=r_1(\cos\theta_1+i\sin\theta_1) \]

and

\[ z_2=r_2(\cos\theta_2+i\sin\theta_2), \]

then

\[ z_1z_2 = r_1r_2\left(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)+i\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\right). \]

Multiplication multiplies moduli and adds arguments. Division divides moduli and subtracts arguments:

\[ \dfrac{z_1}{z_2} = \dfrac{r_1}{r_2}\left(\cos(\theta_1-\theta_2)+i\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)\right). \]

12.4.3 Worked example

Write

\[ z=-1+\sqrt3 i \]

in polar form.

First,

\[ r=\sqrt{(-1)^2+(\sqrt3)^2}=2. \]

The point lies in Quadrant II, with reference angle

\[ \dfrac{\pi}{3}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \arg z=\dfrac{2\pi}{3}. \]

Hence

\[ z=2\left(\cos\dfrac{2\pi}{3}+i\sin\dfrac{2\pi}{3}\right). \]

12.4.4 Worked example

Let

\[ z=\sqrt3+i \]

and

\[ w=1+i. \]

Find \(zw\) in polar form.

We have

\[ z=2\left(\cos\dfrac{\pi}{6}+i\sin\dfrac{\pi}{6}\right) \]

and

\[ w=\sqrt2\left(\cos\dfrac{\pi}{4}+i\sin\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right). \]

Therefore,

\[ zw=2\sqrt2\left(\cos\dfrac{5\pi}{12}+i\sin\dfrac{5\pi}{12}\right). \]

12.5 12.4 Equations with complex roots

12.5.1 Key mathematical ideas

Complex numbers allow equations such as

\[ x^2+1=0 \]

to have solutions:

\[ x=\pm i. \]

For polynomials with real coefficients, non-real roots occur in conjugate pairs. Therefore, if

\[ 2+3i \]

is a root of a polynomial with real coefficients, then

\[ 2-3i \]

is also a root.

If a quadratic has roots

\[ 2+3i \quad\text{and}\quad 2-3i, \]

then the quadratic is

\[ (x-(2+3i))(x-(2-3i)). \]

This simplifies to

\[ (x-2)^2+9=x^2-4x+13. \]

12.5.3 Worked example

Find the two square roots of

\[ 3+4i. \]

Let

\[ (a+bi)^2=3+4i. \]

Expanding gives

\[ a^2-b^2+2abi=3+4i. \]

Therefore,

\[ a^2-b^2=3 \]

and

\[ 2ab=4. \]

Also, taking moduli gives

\[ a^2+b^2=|3+4i|=5. \]

Adding

\[ a^2-b^2=3 \]

and

\[ a^2+b^2=5 \]

gives

\[ 2a^2=8, \]

so

\[ a=\pm2. \]

Since \(ab=2\), the roots are

\[ 2+i \quad\text{and}\quad -2-i. \]

Therefore,

\[ z=\pm(2+i). \]

12.6 12.5 Loci and shaded regions in the complex plane

12.6.1 Key mathematical ideas

Let

\[ z=x+iy. \]

Then complex equations and inequalities can describe loci in the plane.

A circle locus has the form

\[ |z-(a+bi)|=r. \]

This means the point \(z\) is distance \(r\) from the fixed point \(a+bi\). In Cartesian form,

\[ (x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2. \]

A perpendicular-bisector locus has the form

\[ |z-a|=|z-b|. \]

This means the point \(z\) is equidistant from two fixed points.

An argument locus has the form

\[ \arg(z-a)=\theta. \]

This is a ray starting from the point \(a\), at angle \(\theta\) to the positive real axis. The starting point is excluded because the argument is undefined there.

12.6.5 Worked example

Find the Cartesian equation of

\[ |z-(2+i)|=3. \]

Let

\[ z=x+iy. \]

Then

\[ z-(2+i)=(x-2)+i(y-1). \]

So

\[ |z-(2+i)|=\sqrt{(x-2)^2+(y-1)^2}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \sqrt{(x-2)^2+(y-1)^2}=3. \]

Squaring gives

\[ (x-2)^2+(y-1)^2=9. \]

12.7 12.6 Mixed problem solving

12.7.1 Worked example: intersection of loci

Find all complex numbers satisfying

\[ |z-(3+2i)|=2 \]

and

\[ \arg(z-1)=\dfrac{\pi}{4}. \]

Let

\[ z=x+iy. \]

The circle is

\[ (x-3)^2+(y-2)^2=4. \]

The argument condition means the ray from \(1\) at angle \(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\), so

\[ y=x-1, \quad x>1. \]

Substitute into the circle:

\[ (x-3)^2+(x-3)^2=4. \]

Thus

\[ 2(x-3)^2=4, \]

so

\[ x=3\pm\sqrt2. \]

Since both values satisfy \(x>1\),

\[ y=2\pm\sqrt2. \]

Therefore,

\[ z=(3+\sqrt2)+(2+\sqrt2)i \]

or

\[ z=(3-\sqrt2)+(2-\sqrt2)i. \]

## 12.1 Imaginary numbers and Cartesian form

### Key mathematical ideas

The imaginary unit $i$ is defined by

$$
i^2=-1.
$$

A complex number has Cartesian form

$$
z=x+iy,
$$

where $x$ and $y$ are real numbers.

The real part is

$$
\operatorname{Re}(z)=x,
$$

and the imaginary part is

$$
\operatorname{Im}(z)=y.
$$

Two complex numbers are equal if and only if their real and imaginary parts are equal. Therefore, if

$$
a+bi=c+di,
$$

then

$$
a=c
\quad\text{and}\quad
b=d.
$$

## 12.2 Conjugates, modulus, argument and division

### Key mathematical ideas

If

$$
z=x+iy,
$$

then the conjugate of $z$ is

$$
\bar z=x-iy.
$$

The modulus of $z$ is

$$
|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}.
$$

The argument of $z$ is the angle measured from the positive real axis to the line from the origin to the point representing $z$.

The key identity is

$$
z\bar z=(x+iy)(x-iy)=x^2+y^2=|z|^2.
$$

This is why conjugates are useful for division.

## 12.3 Polar form, multiplication and transformations

### Key mathematical ideas

A complex number can be written in Cartesian form as

$$
z=x+iy.
$$

It can also be written in polar form as

$$
z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta),
$$

where

$$
r=|z|
$$

and

$$
\theta=\arg z.
$$

If

$$
z_1=r_1(\cos\theta_1+i\sin\theta_1)
$$

and

$$
z_2=r_2(\cos\theta_2+i\sin\theta_2),
$$

then

$$
z_1z_2
=
r_1r_2\left(\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)+i\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\right).
$$

Multiplication multiplies moduli and adds arguments. Division divides moduli and subtracts arguments:

$$
\dfrac{z_1}{z_2}
=
\dfrac{r_1}{r_2}\left(\cos(\theta_1-\theta_2)+i\sin(\theta_1-\theta_2)\right).
$$

### Visualisation using R

## 12.4 Equations with complex roots

### Key mathematical ideas

Complex numbers allow equations such as

$$
x^2+1=0
$$

to have solutions:

$$
x=\pm i.
$$

For polynomials with real coefficients, non-real roots occur in conjugate pairs. Therefore, if

$$
2+3i
$$

is a root of a polynomial with real coefficients, then

$$
2-3i
$$

is also a root.

If a quadratic has roots

$$
2+3i
\quad\text{and}\quad
2-3i,
$$

then the quadratic is

$$
(x-(2+3i))(x-(2-3i)).
$$

This simplifies to

$$
(x-2)^2+9=x^2-4x+13.
$$

Therefore,

$$

and

-2-i.

## 12.5 Loci and shaded regions in the complex plane

### Key mathematical ideas

Let

$$
z=x+iy.
$$

Then complex equations and inequalities can describe loci in the plane.

A circle locus has the form

$$
|z-(a+bi)|=r.
$$

This means the point $z$ is distance $r$ from the fixed point $a+bi$. In Cartesian form,

$$
(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2.
$$

A perpendicular-bisector locus has the form

$$
|z-a|=|z-b|.
$$

This means the point $z$ is equidistant from two fixed points.

An argument locus has the form

$$
\arg(z-a)=\theta.
$$

This is a ray starting from the point $a$, at angle $\theta$ to the positive real axis. The starting point is excluded because the argument is undefined there.

### Worked example: intersection of loci

Find all complex numbers satisfying

$$
|z-(3+2i)|=2
$$

$$

$$

$$
z=x+iy.
$$

The circle is

$$
(x-3)^2+(y-2)^2=4.
$$

The argument condition means the ray from $1$ at angle $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$, so

$$
y=x-1,
\quad x>1.
$$

Substitute into the circle:

$$
(x-3)^2+(x-3)^2=4.
$$

Thus

$$
2(x-3)^2=4,
$$

so

$$
x=3\pm\sqrt2.
$$

Since both values satisfy $x>1$,

$$
y=2\pm\sqrt2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
z=(3+\sqrt2)+(2+\sqrt2)i
$$

or

$$
z=(3-\sqrt2)+(2-\sqrt2)i.
$$

### Answers to STEP-style extension questions

1. If $z=x+iy$, then $\bar z=x-iy$. Hence
   $$
   z\bar z=(x+iy)(x-iy)=x^2+y^2=|z|^2.
   $$
   Therefore,
   $$
   \dfrac{a+bi}{c+di}
   =
   \dfrac{(a+bi)(c-di)}{c^2+d^2}
   =
   \dfrac{ac+bd}{c^2+d^2}+\dfrac{bc-ad}{c^2+d^2}i.
   $$

2. Let $z=a+bi$. Then $a^2-b^2=3$ and $2ab=4$. Also $a^2+b^2=5$. Hence $a^2=4$ and $ab=2$, giving $z=2+i$ or $z=-2-i$. These are opposite points on the Argand diagram because one is the negative of the other.

3. Write $w=s(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi)$. Then
   $$
   zw=rs(\cos(\theta+\phi)+i\sin(\theta+\phi)).
   $$
   So the modulus is multiplied by $r$ and the argument is increased by $\theta$.

4. The ray gives $y=x-1$ with $x>1$. Substitution into $(x-3)^2+(y-2)^2=4$ gives $2(x-3)^2=4$, so $x=3\pm\sqrt2$. Therefore
   $$
   z=(3+\sqrt2)+(2+\sqrt2)i
   $$
   or
   $$
   z=(3-\sqrt2)+(2-\sqrt2)i.
   $$

5. The circle has centre $(5,5)$ and radius $5$. The line from the origin to the centre has argument $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$ and length $5\sqrt2$. The tangent angle satisfies
   $$
   \sin\alpha=\dfrac{5}{5\sqrt2}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt2},
   $$
   so $\alpha=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$. Hence the least argument is $0$ and the greatest argument is $\dfrac{\pi}{2}$.
