11.1 Overview

This chapter develops six connected differential-equation ideas:

  1. forming differential equations from statements about rates of change;
  2. solving separable differential equations;
  3. using initial conditions to find particular solutions;
  4. modelling growth, decay and saturation;
  5. modelling cooling and weather-type relaxation;
  6. interpreting deterministic models in contexts such as finance and environmental data.

The central technique in this chapter is separating the variables. We will not use integrating factors, second-order differential equations, or stochastic differential equations as solution techniques.

11.2 Inspiration: differential equations behind real-world data

Differential equations are not just symbolic exercises. They are one of the main languages used to describe change in the real world.

In this chapter, our technique is deliberately limited to separating the variables. However, the same basic idea sits underneath much more sophisticated models used in weather forecasting, finance, population modelling, epidemics, cooling, chemical reactions and environmental science.

The guiding question is:

If we know how fast something changes, can we predict what happens next?

Real applications usually begin with data. For example:

  • weather observations from New Zealand and Vietnam;
  • temperature, rainfall, wind and sunshine summaries;
  • exchange-rate data such as NZD/VND or USD/VND;
  • population or environmental indices;
  • school-based data such as attendance, enrolment, revision completion or assessment readiness.

The full real-world models may be complicated. But in this chapter we study simplified models that still capture the same central idea: a rate of change determines a future state.

The important point is that our simple models are not the whole story. They are entry points into the same mathematical way of thinking.

11.2.2 Example 1: weather and climate

Modern weather forecasting does not use one simple equation. A full numerical weather model may involve velocity, pressure, temperature, moisture, radiation, surface effects and the rotation of the Earth.

A simplified momentum equation may contain terms such as

\[ \dfrac{D\mathbf{u}}{Dt} + f\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{u} = -\dfrac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \mathbf{g} + \nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}. \]

This is far beyond our course. It is a partial differential equation involving vectors, pressure gradients, rotation and diffusion.

But a simple classroom version might ask:

A hot surface cools towards the surrounding air temperature. How does its temperature change?

That leads to the separable model

\[ \dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A), \]

where \(A\) is the ambient temperature.

This simple equation already captures an important real-world behaviour: the further \(T\) is from \(A\), the faster it changes.

11.2.3 Example 2: finance and exchange rates

Financial data such as NZD/VND or USD/VND exchange rates do not move smoothly. They respond to interest rates, trade, policy, news, expectations and market shocks.

A more advanced stochastic model may be written as

\[ dS_t=\mu S_t\,dt+\sigma S_t\,dW_t. \]

Here, the term

\[ \mu S_t\,dt \]

represents the deterministic trend, while

\[ \sigma S_t\,dW_t \]

represents random fluctuation.

Stochastic differential equations are not part of this chapter. However, if we ignore the random term temporarily, we get the separable deterministic model

\[ \dfrac{dS}{dt}=\mu S. \]

Solving this gives

\[ S=S_0e^{\mu t}. \]

So the model in this chapter is a simplified backbone for a much more complex financial idea.

11.2.4 Example 3: epidemics and saturation

A simple early-stage epidemic model may assume that the number of infected individuals grows at a rate proportional to the current number infected:

\[ \dfrac{dI}{dt}=kI. \]

This gives exponential growth:

\[ I=I_0e^{kt}. \]

But unlimited exponential growth is not realistic. In a fixed population, growth slows down as the number of susceptible people decreases.

A more realistic model may involve several coupled equations, such as a susceptible-infected-recovered system. A simplified version in this chapter is logistic growth:

\[ \dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP\left(1-\dfrac{P}{M}\right). \]

This is still separable, but it captures a better idea: growth is fast at first, then slows as the quantity approaches a maximum capacity \(M\).

11.2.6 Real-world example: using a differential equation to model an NZ bank-related share price

Heartland Group Holdings Limited is listed on the NZX under the ticker HGH. Heartland describes its story as one involving specialist finance and banking products, so this gives us a useful New Zealand financial context for a differential-equations model.

The NZX page for HGH gives the following delayed market data:

Code 
hgh_data <- data.frame(
  source = "NZX delayed market data",
  ticker = "HGH",
  company = "Heartland Group Holdings Limited",
  data_timestamp = "27/06/2026, 16:31 NZST",
  current_price_nzd = 1.2050,
  change_52_week_nzd = 0.4350,
  change_52_week_percent = 56.49
)

hgh_data

This gives us two real data points for a simple classroom model.

The current price is approximately

\[ S(1)=1.205. \]

The 52-week change is approximately

\[ 0.435. \]

So the approximate price one year earlier was

\[ S(0)=1.205-0.435=0.770. \]

We now model the share price \(S\) using the separable differential equation

\[ \dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS, \]

where \(t\) is measured in years from approximately 52 weeks ago.

Separating the variables gives

\[ \dfrac{1}{S}\dfrac{dS}{dt}=r. \]

So

\[ \int \dfrac{1}{S}\,dS=\int r\,dt. \]

Hence

\[ \ln S=rt+C. \]

Therefore,

\[ S=S_0e^{rt}. \]

Using the real NZX data,

\[ S(0)=0.770 \]

and

\[ S(1)=1.205. \]

So

\[ 1.205=0.770e^r. \]

Therefore,

\[ e^r=\dfrac{1.205}{0.770}. \]

Taking logarithms gives

\[ r=\ln\left(\dfrac{1.205}{0.770}\right). \]

Thus

\[ r\approx0.448. \]

The fitted differential-equation model is

\[ S(t)=0.770e^{0.448t}. \]

Warning

This is not investment advice and not a genuine financial forecast. It is a mathematical modelling example using two real NZX data points. Real share prices are affected by earnings, interest rates, dividends, credit conditions, market sentiment, news and random shocks.

11.2.8 A light prediction

A full one-year extrapolation would be too aggressive for a classroom model based on only two data points. Instead, we make a light 13-week projection.

Since

\[ 13\text{ weeks}=\dfrac{13}{52}\text{ years}=0.25\text{ years}, \]

we estimate

\[ S(1.25)=1.205e^{0.448(0.25)}. \]

This gives

\[ S(1.25)\approx1.348. \]

So the simple model gives a light 13-week projection of approximately

\[ \$1.35. \]

This should be interpreted as a mathematical projection, not a financial prediction.

prediction_weeks <- c(4, 8, 13)

11.2.10 Scenario check

Because the fitted value

\[ r\approx0.448 \]

comes from a strong 52-week movement, it is risky to assume that this rate continues. A more honest modelling approach is to compare scenarios.

  years_after_current_price = future_time,

    "same fitted rate",

11.2.11 Interpretation

This example follows the modelling cycle:

\[ \text{real NZX data} \quad\longrightarrow\quad \dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS \quad\longrightarrow\quad S=S_0e^{rt} \quad\longrightarrow\quad \text{fit }r \quad\longrightarrow\quad \text{make a cautious projection}. \]

The strength of the differential equation is that the assumption is explicit:

The price changes at a rate proportional to the current price.

Once that assumption is made, the mathematics forces an exponential model. The final step is not just calculation, but critique: we ask whether the assumption is reasonable.

11.2.12 Why we still study simple separable models

A simple separable differential equation is useful because it teaches the core modelling cycle:

  1. identify the changing quantity;
  2. describe its rate of change;
  3. write a differential equation;
  4. separate the variables;
  5. integrate;
  6. use an initial condition;
  7. interpret the solution in context.

The models in this chapter are simplified, but they are not artificial. They are the first layer of the same mathematical thinking used in larger models.

For example,

\[ \dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A) \]

is not a full weather model, but it captures relaxation towards an ambient value.

Similarly,

\[ \dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS \]

is not a full financial model, but it captures the deterministic backbone of continuous growth or decay.

And

\[ \dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP\left(1-\dfrac{P}{M}\right) \]

is not a full population or epidemic model, but it captures the idea that growth slows as a limiting capacity is approached.

This chapter therefore acts as a bridge:

\[ \text{data} \quad\longrightarrow\quad \text{rate of change} \quad\longrightarrow\quad \text{differential equation} \quad\longrightarrow\quad \text{solution} \quad\longrightarrow\quad \text{interpretation}. \]

11.3 10.1 Forming differential equations from rates of change

11.3.1 Key mathematical ideas

A differential equation describes how a quantity changes.

If a quantity \(y\) changes with respect to \(x\), then its rate of change is

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}. \]

Many modelling statements translate directly into differential equations.

If \(y\) increases at a rate proportional to \(y\), then

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=ky, \]

where \(k>0\).

If \(y\) decreases at a rate proportional to \(y\), then

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=-ky, \]

where \(k>0\).

If a temperature \(T\) approaches an ambient temperature \(A\), then a common model is

\[ \dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A), \]

where \(k>0\).

This means the rate of temperature change depends on the difference between the current temperature and the ambient temperature.

11.3.3 Worked example

A cup of tea has temperature \(T\) degrees Celsius after \(t\) minutes. The surrounding air temperature is \(22^\circ\text{C}\). The tea cools at a rate proportional to how much hotter it is than the surrounding air.

Form a differential equation.

The temperature difference is

\[ T-22. \]

Since the tea is cooling, the rate of change is negative when \(T>22\).

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-22), \quad k>0. \]

This equation also has the sensible equilibrium

\[ T=22, \]

because when \(T=22\),

\[ \dfrac{dT}{dt}=0. \]

11.4 10.2 Solving separable differential equations

11.4.1 Key mathematical ideas

A first-order differential equation is separable if it can be rearranged so that all the \(y\) terms are on one side and all the \(x\) terms are on the other.

For example,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy \]

can be rearranged as

\[ \dfrac{1}{y}\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x. \]

This leads to

\[ \int \dfrac{1}{y}\,dy=\int x\,dx. \]

So

\[ \ln|y|=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C. \]

A common final form is

\[ y=Ae^{\dfrac{x^2}{2}}, \]

where \(A\) is a non-zero constant.

11.4.3 Worked example

Solve

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=2xy. \]

Separate the variables:

\[ \dfrac{1}{y}\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2x. \]

Integrate both sides:

\[ \int \dfrac{1}{y}\,dy = \int 2x\,dx. \]

So

\[ \ln|y|=x^2+C. \]

Exponentiating gives

\[ |y|=e^{x^2+C}=e^C e^{x^2}. \]

So the general solution may be written as

\[ y=Ae^{x^2}, \]

where \(A\) is a constant.

11.5 10.3 Particular solutions and interpretation

11.5.1 Key mathematical ideas

A general solution contains an arbitrary constant.

A particular solution uses extra information, usually an initial condition, to find that constant.

For example, the general solution

\[ y=Ae^{x^2} \]

becomes a particular solution if we know the value of \(y\) at one point.

If

\[ y(0)=7, \]

then

\[ 7=Ae^0=A. \]

So the particular solution is

\[ y=7e^{x^2}. \]

Integrate:

\[ 2=A. \]

Therefore,

\[ y=2(1+x)^3, \quad x>-1. \]

11.6 10.4 Growth, decay and saturation models

11.6.1 Key mathematical ideas

The simplest growth model is

\[ \dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP, \quad k>0. \]

Separating variables gives

\[ \dfrac{1}{P}\dfrac{dP}{dt}=k. \]

So

\[ \ln P=kt+C, \]

and therefore

\[ P=P_0e^{kt}. \]

The simplest decay model is

\[ \dfrac{dP}{dt}=-kP, \quad k>0, \]

with solution

\[ P=P_0e^{-kt}. \]

Growth proportional to current amount is a useful first model, but it cannot grow forever in a restricted environment. A saturation model has the form

\[ \dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP\left(1-\dfrac{P}{M}\right), \]

where \(M\) is the carrying capacity.

11.6.3 Worked example

A quantity \(P\) grows at a rate proportional to \(P\). Initially,

\[ P(0)=500. \]

After \(10\) years,

\[ P(10)=700. \]

Find a model for \(P\).

Since

\[ \dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP, \]

we have

\[ P=500e^{kt}. \]

Using \(P(10)=700\) gives

\[ 700=500e^{10k}. \]

Thus

\[ e^{10k}=\dfrac{7}{5}. \]

So

\[ 10k=\ln\left(\dfrac{7}{5}\right), \]

and

\[ k=\dfrac{1}{10}\ln\left(\dfrac{7}{5}\right). \]

Therefore,

\[ P=500e^{\dfrac{t}{10}\ln(\dfrac{7}{5})}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ P=500\left(\dfrac{7}{5}\right)^{t/10}. \]

11.7 10.5 Cooling and weather-type relaxation models

11.7.1 Key mathematical ideas

Newton’s law of cooling has the form

\[ \dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A), \]

where:

  • \(T\) is the object’s temperature;
  • \(A\) is the ambient temperature;
  • \(k>0\) is the cooling constant.

This model is separable:

\[ \dfrac{1}{T-A}\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k. \]

So

\[ \ln|T-A|=-kt+C. \]

Therefore,

\[ T=A+Ce^{-kt}. \]

If \(T(0)=T_0\), then

\[ T=A+(T_0-A)e^{-kt}. \]

This form also models many relaxation processes: a quantity moves gradually towards a long-term level.

11.7.3 Worked example

A hot drink is placed in a room at temperature

\[ 26^\circ\text{C}. \]

Initially, the drink is at

\[ 80^\circ\text{C}. \]

After \(10\) minutes, its temperature is

\[ 50^\circ\text{C}. \]

Assume

\[ \dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-26). \]

Find \(T\) in terms of \(t\).

The general solution is

\[ T=26+Ce^{-kt}. \]

Using \(T(0)=80\) gives

\[ 80=26+C, \]

so

\[ C=54. \]

Thus

\[ T=26+54e^{-kt}. \]

Using \(T(10)=50\) gives

\[ 50=26+54e^{-10k}. \]

So

\[ 24=54e^{-10k}, \]

and

\[ e^{-10k}=\dfrac{4}{9}. \]

Taking logarithms,

\[ -10k=\ln\left(\dfrac{4}{9}\right). \]

Hence

\[ k=\dfrac{1}{10}\ln\left(\dfrac{9}{4}\right). \]

Therefore,

\[ T=26+54e^{-\dfrac{t}{10}\ln(\dfrac{9}{4})}. \]

11.8 10.6 Deterministic finance models and stochastic context

11.8.1 Key mathematical ideas

A simple deterministic finance model assumes a value \(S\) changes at a rate proportional to itself:

\[ \dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS. \]

Separating variables gives

\[ \dfrac{1}{S}\dfrac{dS}{dt}=r. \]

Thus

\[ \ln S=rt+C, \]

and

\[ S=S_0e^{rt}. \]

This is the deterministic backbone behind many compound-growth models.

Real financial data are not smooth. Exchange rates, asset prices and interest-rate-sensitive values include random shocks. A stochastic model may add a random component, but stochastic differential equations are beyond this chapter.

In this chapter, we use only the deterministic separable model.

## Inspiration: differential equations behind real-world data

Differential equations are not just symbolic exercises. They are one of the main languages used to describe change in the real world.

In this chapter, our technique is deliberately limited to **separating the variables**. However, the same basic idea sits underneath much more sophisticated models used in weather forecasting, finance, population modelling, epidemics, cooling, chemical reactions and environmental science.

The guiding question is:

> If we know how fast something changes, can we predict what happens next?

Real applications usually begin with data. For example:

- weather observations from New Zealand and Vietnam;
- temperature, rainfall, wind and sunshine summaries;
- exchange-rate data such as NZD/VND or USD/VND;
- population or environmental indices;
- school-based data such as attendance, enrolment, revision completion or assessment readiness.

The full real-world models may be complicated. But in this chapter we study simplified models that still capture the same central idea: **a rate of change determines a future state**.

### From real-world systems to differential equations

| Context | Real-world data students can recognise | More advanced differential-equation model | Separable model used in this chapter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weather forecasting | Temperature, rainfall, wind speed, pressure | Coupled nonlinear partial differential equations from fluid dynamics and thermodynamics | $\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A)$ |
| Cooling and heating | Tea cooling in a room, classroom temperature, outdoor/indoor comparison | Heat equation or energy-balance models | $\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A)$ |
| Population growth | Population index, school roll, bacteria growth | Logistic or multi-compartment systems | $\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP\left(1-\dfrac{P}{M}\right)$ |
| Finance | Exchange rates, investment indices, price series | Stochastic differential equations with random noise | $\dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS$ |
| Epidemics | Number infected, recovered or susceptible | Coupled SIR-type differential equations | $\dfrac{dI}{dt}=kI$ or $\dfrac{dI}{dt}=kI(M-I)$ |
| Environmental recovery | Pollution level, rainfall deficit, concentration decay | Transport-diffusion-reaction equations | $\dfrac{dC}{dt}=-kC$ |

The important point is that our simple models are not the whole story. They are **entry points** into the same mathematical way of thinking.

### Example 1: weather and climate

Modern weather forecasting does not use one simple equation. A full numerical weather model may involve velocity, pressure, temperature, moisture, radiation, surface effects and the rotation of the Earth.

A simplified momentum equation may contain terms such as

$$
\dfrac{D\mathbf{u}}{Dt}
+
f\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{u}
=
-\dfrac{1}{\rho}\nabla p
+
\mathbf{g}
+
\nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}.
$$

This is far beyond our course. It is a partial differential equation involving vectors, pressure gradients, rotation and diffusion.

But a simple classroom version might ask:

> A hot surface cools towards the surrounding air temperature. How does its temperature change?

That leads to the separable model

$$
\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A),
$$

where $A$ is the ambient temperature.

This simple equation already captures an important real-world behaviour: the further $T$ is from $A$, the faster it changes.

### Example 2: finance and exchange rates

Financial data such as NZD/VND or USD/VND exchange rates do not move smoothly. They respond to interest rates, trade, policy, news, expectations and market shocks.

A more advanced stochastic model may be written as

$$
dS_t=\mu S_t\,dt+\sigma S_t\,dW_t.
$$

Here, the term

$$
\mu S_t\,dt
$$

represents the deterministic trend, while

$$
\sigma S_t\,dW_t
$$

represents random fluctuation.

Stochastic differential equations are not part of this chapter. However, if we ignore the random term temporarily, we get the separable deterministic model

$$
\dfrac{dS}{dt}=\mu S.
$$

Solving this gives

$$
S=S_0e^{\mu t}.
$$

So the model in this chapter is a simplified backbone for a much more complex financial idea.

### Example 3: epidemics and saturation

A simple early-stage epidemic model may assume that the number of infected individuals grows at a rate proportional to the current number infected:

$$
\dfrac{dI}{dt}=kI.
$$

This gives exponential growth:

$$
I=I_0e^{kt}.
$$

But unlimited exponential growth is not realistic. In a fixed population, growth slows down as the number of susceptible people decreases.

A more realistic model may involve several coupled equations, such as a susceptible-infected-recovered system. A simplified version in this chapter is logistic growth:

$$
\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP\left(1-\dfrac{P}{M}\right).
$$

This is still separable, but it captures a better idea: growth is fast at first, then slows as the quantity approaches a maximum capacity $M$.

This gives us two real data points for a simple classroom model.

The current price is approximately

$$
S(1)=1.205.
$$

The 52-week change is approximately

$$
0.435.
$$

So the approximate price one year earlier was

$$
S(0)=1.205-0.435=0.770.
$$

We now model the share price $S$ using the separable differential equation

$$
\dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS,
$$

where $t$ is measured in years from approximately 52 weeks ago.

Separating the variables gives

$$
\dfrac{1}{S}\dfrac{dS}{dt}=r.
$$

So

$$
\int \dfrac{1}{S}\,dS=\int r\,dt.
$$

Hence

$$
\ln S=rt+C.
$$

Therefore,

$$
S=S_0e^{rt}.
$$

Using the real NZX data,

$$
S(0)=0.770
$$

and

$$
S(1)=1.205.
$$

So

$$
1.205=0.770e^r.
$$

Therefore,

$$
e^r=\dfrac{1.205}{0.770}.
$$

Taking logarithms gives

$$
r=\ln\left(\dfrac{1.205}{0.770}\right).
$$

Thus

$$
r\approx0.448.
$$

The fitted differential-equation model is

$$
S(t)=0.770e^{0.448t}.
$$

This is not investment advice and not a genuine financial forecast. It is a mathematical modelling example using two real NZX data points. Real share prices are affected by earnings, interest rates, dividends, credit conditions, market sentiment, news and random shocks.

### Interpretation

This example follows the modelling cycle:

$$
\text{real NZX data}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
S=S_0e^{rt}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{fit }r
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{make a cautious projection}.
$$

The strength of the differential equation is that the assumption is explicit:

> The price changes at a rate proportional to the current price.

Once that assumption is made, the mathematics forces an exponential model. The final step is not just calculation, but critique: we ask whether the assumption is reasonable.

### Why we still study simple separable models

A simple separable differential equation is useful because it teaches the core modelling cycle:

1. identify the changing quantity;
2. describe its rate of change;
3. write a differential equation;
4. separate the variables;
5. integrate;
6. use an initial condition;
7. interpret the solution in context.

The models in this chapter are simplified, but they are not artificial. They are the first layer of the same mathematical thinking used in larger models.

For example,

$$
\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A)
$$

is not a full weather model, but it captures relaxation towards an ambient value.

Similarly,

$$
\dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS
$$

is not a full financial model, but it captures the deterministic backbone of continuous growth or decay.

And

$$
\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP\left(1-\dfrac{P}{M}\right)
$$

is not a full population or epidemic model, but it captures the idea that growth slows as a limiting capacity is approached.

This chapter therefore acts as a bridge:

$$
\text{data}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{rate of change}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{differential equation}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad
\text{solution}
\quad\longrightarrow\quad

## 10.1 Forming differential equations from rates of change

### Key mathematical ideas

A differential equation describes how a quantity changes.

If a quantity $y$ changes with respect to $x$, then its rate of change is

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}.
$$

Many modelling statements translate directly into differential equations.

If $y$ increases at a rate proportional to $y$, then

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}=ky,
$$

where $k>0$.

If $y$ decreases at a rate proportional to $y$, then

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-ky,
$$

where $k>0$.

If a temperature $T$ approaches an ambient temperature $A$, then a common model is

$$
\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A),
$$

where $k>0$.

## 10.2 Solving separable differential equations

### Key mathematical ideas

A first-order differential equation is **separable** if it can be rearranged so that all the $y$ terms are on one side and all the $x$ terms are on the other.

For example,

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}=xy
$$

can be rearranged as

$$
\dfrac{1}{y}\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x.
$$

This leads to

$$
\int \dfrac{1}{y}\,dy=\int x\,dx.
$$

So

$$
\ln|y|=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C.
$$

A common final form is

$$
y=Ae^{\dfrac{x^2}{2}},
$$

where $A$ is a non-zero constant.

### Worked example

Solve

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2xy.
$$

Separate the variables:

$$
\dfrac{1}{y}\dfrac{dy}{dx}=2x.
$$

Integrate both sides:

$$
\int \dfrac{1}{y}\,dy
=
\int 2x\,dx.
$$

So

$$
\ln|y|=x^2+C.
$$

Exponentiating gives

$$
|y|=e^{x^2+C}=e^C e^{x^2}.
$$

So the general solution may be written as

$$
y=Ae^{x^2},
$$

where $A$ is a constant.

## 10.3 Particular solutions and interpretation

### Key mathematical ideas

A **general solution** contains an arbitrary constant.

A **particular solution** uses extra information, usually an initial condition, to find that constant.

For example, the general solution

$$
y=Ae^{x^2}
$$

becomes a particular solution if we know the value of $y$ at one point.

If

$$
y(0)=7,
$$

then

$$
7=Ae^0=A.
$$

So the particular solution is

$$
y=7e^{x^2}.
$$

## 10.4 Growth, decay and saturation models

### Key mathematical ideas

The simplest growth model is

$$
\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP,
\quad k>0.
$$

Separating variables gives

$$
\dfrac{1}{P}\dfrac{dP}{dt}=k.
$$

So

$$
\ln P=kt+C,
$$

and therefore

$$
P=P_0e^{kt}.
$$

The simplest decay model is

$$
\dfrac{dP}{dt}=-kP,
\quad k>0,
$$

with solution

$$
P=P_0e^{-kt}.
$$

Growth proportional to current amount is a useful first model, but it cannot grow forever in a restricted environment. A saturation model has the form

$$
\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP\left(1-\dfrac{P}{M}\right),
$$

where $M$ is the carrying capacity.

### Worked example

A quantity $P$ grows at a rate proportional to $P$. Initially,

$$
P(0)=500.
$$

After $10$ years,

$$
P(10)=700.
$$

Find a model for $P$.

Since

$$
\dfrac{dP}{dt}=kP,
$$

we have

$$
P=500e^{kt}.
$$

Using $P(10)=700$ gives

$$
700=500e^{10k}.
$$

Thus

$$
e^{10k}=\dfrac{7}{5}.
$$

So

$$
10k=\ln\left(\dfrac{7}{5}\right),
$$

and

$$
k=\dfrac{1}{10}\ln\left(\dfrac{7}{5}\right).
$$

Therefore,

$$
P=500e^{\dfrac{t}{10}\ln(\dfrac{7}{5})}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
P=500\left(\dfrac{7}{5}\right)^{t/10}.
$$

## 10.5 Cooling and weather-type relaxation models

### Key mathematical ideas

Newton's law of cooling has the form

$$
\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-A),
$$

where:

- $T$ is the object's temperature;
- $A$ is the ambient temperature;
- $k>0$ is the cooling constant.

This model is separable:

$$
\dfrac{1}{T-A}\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k.
$$

So

$$
\ln|T-A|=-kt+C.
$$

Therefore,

$$
T=A+Ce^{-kt}.
$$

If $T(0)=T_0$, then

$$
T=A+(T_0-A)e^{-kt}.
$$

This form also models many **relaxation** processes: a quantity moves gradually towards a long-term level.

### Worked example

A hot drink is placed in a room at temperature

$$

$$

Initially, the drink is at

$$
80^\circ\text{C}.
$$

After $10$ minutes, its temperature is

$$
50^\circ\text{C}.
$$

Assume

$$
\dfrac{dT}{dt}=-k(T-26).
$$

Find $T$ in terms of $t$.

The general solution is

$$
T=26+Ce^{-kt}.
$$

Using $T(0)=80$ gives

$$
80=26+C,
$$

so

$$
C=54.
$$

Thus

$$
T=26+54e^{-kt}.
$$

Using $T(10)=50$ gives

$$
50=26+54e^{-10k}.
$$

So

$$
24=54e^{-10k},
$$

and

$$
e^{-10k}=\dfrac{4}{9}.
$$

Taking logarithms,

$$
-10k=\ln\left(\dfrac{4}{9}\right).
$$

Hence

$$
k=\dfrac{1}{10}\ln\left(\dfrac{9}{4}\right).
$$

Therefore,

$$
T=26+54e^{-\dfrac{t}{10}\ln(\dfrac{9}{4})}.
$$

## 10.6 Deterministic finance models and stochastic context

### Key mathematical ideas

A simple deterministic finance model assumes a value $S$ changes at a rate proportional to itself:

$$
\dfrac{dS}{dt}=rS.
$$

Separating variables gives

$$
\dfrac{1}{S}\dfrac{dS}{dt}=r.
$$

Thus

$$
\ln S=rt+C,
$$

and

$$
S=S_0e^{rt}.
$$

This is the deterministic backbone behind many compound-growth models.

Real financial data are not smooth. Exchange rates, asset prices and interest-rate-sensitive values include random shocks. A stochastic model may add a random component, but stochastic differential equations are beyond this chapter.

In this chapter, we use only the deterministic separable model.

### Visualisation using R

```{r}

#| fig-cap: "A deterministic exponential curve with simulated noisy paths."
#| fig-width: 6
#| fig-height: 4

  time = time_values,
  rate_value = growth_solution(time_values, initial_rate, annual_drift),
  path = "deterministic"

  rbind,
  lapply(1:6, function(path_id) {
    random_steps <- rnorm(length(time_values) - 1, mean = 0, sd = sqrt(1 / 252))
    log_values <- numeric(length(time_values))
    log_values[1] <- log(initial_rate)
    
    for (i in 2:length(time_values)) {
      log_values[i] <- log_values[i - 1] +
        (annual_drift - 0.5 * annual_volatility^2) / 252 +
        annual_volatility * random_steps[i - 1]
    }
```

### Worked example

A deterministic exchange-rate index $S$ satisfies

$$
\dfrac{dS}{dt}=0.02S,
$$

where $t$ is measured in years.

Suppose

$$
S(0)=15000.
$$

Find $S(t)$ and estimate $S(0.5)$.

Since

$$
\dfrac{dS}{dt}=0.02S,
$$

we have

$$
S=15000e^{0.02t}.
$$

At $t=0.5$,

$$
S(0.5)=15000e^{0.01}.
$$

Thus

$$
S(0.5)\approx15150.75.
$$

This is not a forecast. It is a simple deterministic model for practising separable differential equations.

### Answers to STEP-style extension questions

1. Separating variables gives
   $$
   \int \dfrac{1}{y}\,dy=\int x\,dx.
   $$
   Hence
   $$
   \ln|y|=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C.
   $$
   Since $y(0)=2$,
   $$
   y=2e^{x^2/2}.
   $$
   Differentiating gives
   $$
   \dfrac{dy}{dx}=2e^{x^2/2}\cdot x=xy.
   $$

2. Separate variables:
   $$
   \int \dfrac{1}{(y-1)^2}\,dy=\int x\,dx.
   $$
   Thus
   $$
   -\dfrac{1}{y-1}=\dfrac{x^2}{2}+C.
   $$
   Since $y(0)=2$, we get $C=-1$. Hence
   $$
   \dfrac{1}{y-1}=1-\dfrac{x^2}{2}.
   $$
   Therefore,
   $$
   y=1+\dfrac{1}{1-\dfrac{x^2}{2}}.
   $$
   The largest interval containing $0$ is
   $$
   -\sqrt2<x<\sqrt2.
   $$

3. Separate variables:
   $$
   \dfrac{dP}{P(1-P/M)}=k\,dt.
   $$
   Since
   $$
   \dfrac{1}{P(1-P/M)}=\dfrac{M}{P(M-P)}
   =
   \dfrac{1}{P}+\dfrac{1}{M-P},
   $$
   integration gives
   $$
   \ln|P|-\ln|M-P|=kt+C.
   $$
   Hence
   $$
   \ln\left|\dfrac{P}{M-P}\right|=kt+C.
   $$
   Therefore
   $$
   \dfrac{P}{M-P}=Be^{kt}.
   $$
   Rearranging gives
   $$
   P=\dfrac{M}{1+Ae^{-kt}}.
   $$

4. Separating variables gives
   $$
   \int \dfrac{1}{T-A}\,dT=\int -k\,dt.
   $$
   Hence
   $$
   \ln|T-A|=-kt+C.
   $$
   Therefore
   $$
   T=A+Ce^{-kt}.
   $$
   Using $T(0)=T_0$ gives $C=T_0-A$, so
   $$
   T=A+(T_0-A)e^{-kt}.
   $$
   Since $e^{-kt}>0$ for finite $t$, the model approaches $A$ but does not reach it in finite time unless $T_0=A$.

5. Since
   $$
   S=S_0e^{rt},
   $$
   the two conditions give
   $$
   18000=S_0e^r
   $$
   and
   $$
   22000=S_0e^{3r}.
   $$
   Dividing gives
   $$
   \dfrac{11}{9}=e^{2r}.
   $$
   Hence
   $$
   r=\dfrac{1}{2}\ln\left(\dfrac{11}{9}\right).
   $$
   Also
   $$
   S_0=18000e^{-r}
   =
   18000\sqrt{\dfrac{9}{11}}.
   $$
