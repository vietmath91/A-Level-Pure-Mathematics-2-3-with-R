5.1 Overview

This chapter develops seven connected differentiation ideas:

  1. the product rule;
  2. the quotient rule;
  3. derivatives of exponential functions;
  4. derivatives of natural logarithmic functions;
  5. derivatives of trigonometric functions;
  6. implicit differentiation;
  7. parametric differentiation.

The central aim is to extend differentiation beyond simple powers of \(x\). We now differentiate products, quotients, exponential functions, logarithmic functions, trigonometric functions, implicit curves and parametric curves.

Note

The exercises are organised into tiers.

Bronze develops fluency.
Silver develops simple application.
Gold develops exam-style reasoning.

5.2 4.1 The product rule

5.2.1 Key mathematical ideas

Suppose

\[ y=uv, \]

where \(u\) and \(v\) are both functions of \(x\).

Then the product rule is

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = u\dfrac{dv}{dx} + v\dfrac{du}{dx}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}(uv)=u'v+uv'. \]

The product rule is needed when a function is naturally written as the product of two differentiable functions.

5.2.2 Visualisation using R

Code 
f <- function(x) x^2 * exp(x)

df <- data.frame(
  x = seq(-3, 2, length.out = 600)
)

df$y <- f(df$x)
df$exact_derivative <- exp(df$x) * (df$x^2 + 2 * df$x)
df$numeric_derivative <- numeric_derivative(f, df$x)

ggplot(df, aes(x = x)) +
  geom_line(aes(y = exact_derivative, linetype = "exact derivative"), linewidth = 1.1) +
  geom_line(aes(y = numeric_derivative, linetype = "numerical derivative"), linewidth = 1.1) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0) +
  labs(
    title = "Derivative of y = x^2 e^x",
    subtitle = "The product rule agrees with numerical gradient estimates.",
    x = "x",
    y = "gradient",
    linetype = "Method"
  ) +
  book_theme()

5.2.3 Worked example

Differentiate

\[ y=x^2e^x. \]

Let

\[ u=x^2 \]

and

\[ v=e^x. \]

Then

\[ \dfrac{du}{dx}=2x \]

and

\[ \dfrac{dv}{dx}=e^x. \]

Using the product rule,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = x^2e^x+e^x(2x). \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = e^x(x^2+2x). \]

5.3 4.2 The quotient rule

5.3.1 Key mathematical ideas

Suppose

\[ y=\dfrac{u}{v}, \]

where \(u\) and \(v\) are differentiable functions of \(x\) and \(v\neq0\).

Then the quotient rule is

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{v\dfrac{du}{dx}-u\dfrac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right) = \dfrac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}. \]

A common error is to differentiate the numerator and denominator separately. In general,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right) \neq \dfrac{u'}{v'}. \]

5.3.3 Worked example

Differentiate

\[ y=\dfrac{x^2+1}{x-1}. \]

Let

\[ u=x^2+1 \]

and

\[ v=x-1. \]

Then

\[ \dfrac{du}{dx}=2x \]

and

\[ \dfrac{dv}{dx}=1. \]

Using the quotient rule,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{(x-1)(2x)-(x^2+1)(1)}{(x-1)^2}. \]

Simplifying the numerator,

\[ (x-1)(2x)-(x^2+1) = 2x^2-2x-x^2-1 = x^2-2x-1. \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{x^2-2x-1}{(x-1)^2}. \]

5.4 4.3 Derivatives of exponential functions

5.4.1 Key mathematical ideas

The natural exponential function has the special property

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(e^x\right)=e^x. \]

Using the chain rule,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(e^{f(x)}\right)=f'(x)e^{f(x)}. \]

In particular,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(e^{ax+b}\right)=ae^{ax+b}. \]

5.5 4.4 Derivatives of natural logarithmic functions

5.5.1 Key mathematical ideas

The derivative of the natural logarithm is

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}(\ln x)=\dfrac{1}{x}, \quad x>0. \]

Using the chain rule,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\ln f(x)\right) = \dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}. \]

In particular,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\ln(ax+b)\right) = \dfrac{a}{ax+b}. \]

The argument of a logarithm must be positive.

    x = "x",

  ) +

5.5.3 Worked example

Differentiate

\[ y=\ln(2x+5). \]

Let

\[ f(x)=2x+5. \]

Then

\[ f'(x)=2. \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{2}{2x+5}. \]

5.6 4.5 Derivatives of trigonometric functions

5.6.1 Key mathematical ideas

For trigonometric differentiation, angles must be measured in radians.

The key derivatives are

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}(\sin x)=\cos x, \]

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}(\cos x)=-\sin x, \]

and

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}(\tan x)=\sec^2x. \]

Using the chain rule,

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\sin(ax+b)\right)=a\cos(ax+b), \]

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\cos(ax+b)\right)=-a\sin(ax+b), \]

and

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\tan(ax+b)\right)=a\sec^2(ax+b). \]

5.7 4.6 Implicit differentiation

5.7.1 Key mathematical ideas

Implicit differentiation is used when a curve is not written directly in the form

\[ y=f(x). \]

For example,

\[ x^2+y^2=25 \]

defines a circle. We differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\), remembering that \(y\) is a function of \(x\).

Since

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}(y^2)=2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}, \]

we get

\[ 2x+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0. \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{x}{y}. \]

5.7.2 Visualisation using R

Code 
theta <- seq(0, 2 * pi, length.out = 800)

circle <- data.frame(
  x = 5 * cos(theta),
  y = 5 * sin(theta)
)

tangent <- data.frame(
  x = seq(-1, 6, length.out = 200)
)

tangent$y <- 4 - (3 / 4) * (tangent$x - 3)

ggplot() +
  geom_path(data = circle, aes(x = x, y = y), linewidth = 1.1) +
  geom_line(data = tangent, aes(x = x, y = y), linetype = "dashed", linewidth = 1.1) +
  annotate("point", x = 3, y = 4, size = 3) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 0) +
  coord_equal() +
  labs(
    title = "Implicit differentiation on a circle",
    subtitle = "At (3, 4), the gradient is -3/4.",
    x = "x",
    y = "y"
  ) +
  book_theme()

5.7.3 Worked example

Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for

\[ x^2+xy+y^2=7. \]

Differentiate each term with respect to \(x\).

The derivative of \(x^2\) is \(2x\).

For \(xy\), use the product rule:

\[ \dfrac{d}{dx}(xy)=x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y. \]

The derivative of \(y^2\) is

\[ 2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}. \]

So

\[ 2x+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0. \]

Collecting the \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms gives

\[ (x+2y)\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-(2x+y). \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{2x+y}{x+2y}. \]

5.8 4.7 Parametric differentiation

5.8.1 Key mathematical ideas

A curve may be defined parametrically by

\[ x=f(t), \qquad y=g(t). \]

If both \(x\) and \(y\) depend on \(t\), then

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{\dfrac{dy}{dt}}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}}, \]

provided

\[ \dfrac{dx}{dt}\neq0. \]

This is the chain rule in the form

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dt} = \dfrac{dy}{dx}\dfrac{dx}{dt}. \]

A curve is defined by

\[ x=t^2, \qquad y=t^3. \]

Find \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).

First,

\[ \dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t \]

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dt}=3t^2. \]

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{\dfrac{dy}{dt}}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}} = \dfrac{3t^2}{2t}. \]

For \(t\neq0\),

\[ \dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{3t}{2}. \]

## 4.1 The product rule

### Key mathematical ideas

Suppose

$$
y=uv,
$$

where $u$ and $v$ are both functions of $x$.

Then the product rule is

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}
=
u\dfrac{dv}{dx}
+
v\dfrac{du}{dx}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\dfrac{d}{dx}(uv)=u'v+uv'.
$$

The product rule is needed when a function is naturally written as the product of two differentiable functions.

## 4.2 The quotient rule

### Key mathematical ideas

Suppose

$$
y=\dfrac{u}{v},
$$

where $u$ and $v$ are differentiable functions of $x$ and $v\neq0$.

Then the quotient rule is

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}
=
\dfrac{v\dfrac{du}{dx}-u\dfrac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right)
=
\dfrac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}.
$$

A common error is to differentiate the numerator and denominator separately. In general,

$$
\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right)
\neq
\dfrac{u'}{v'}.
$$

$$

$$

and

$$

Then

$$
\dfrac{du}{dx}=2x
$$

and

$$
\dfrac{dv}{dx}=1.
$$

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}
=
\dfrac{(x-1)(2x)-(x^2+1)(1)}{(x-1)^2}.

(x-1)(2x)-(x^2+1)

2x^2-2x-x^2-1

x^2-2x-1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}
=
\dfrac{x^2-2x-1}{(x-1)^2}.
$$

## 4.6 Implicit differentiation

### Key mathematical ideas

Implicit differentiation is used when a curve is not written directly in the form

$$
y=f(x).
$$

For example,

$$
x^2+y^2=25
$$

defines a circle. We differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, remembering that $y$ is a function of $x$.

Since

$$
\dfrac{d}{dx}(y^2)=2y\dfrac{dy}{dx},
$$

we get

$$
2x+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{x}{y}.
$$

### Visualisation using R

```{r}

  x = 5 * cos(theta),
  y = 5 * sin(theta)

  x = seq(-1, 6, length.out = 200)

  geom_path(data = circle, aes(x = x, y = y), linewidth = 1.1) +

  annotate("point", x = 3, y = 4, size = 3) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 0) +

    title = "Implicit differentiation on a circle",
    subtitle = "At (3, 4), the gradient is -3/4.",
    x = "x",
```

### Worked example

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ for

$$
x^2+xy+y^2=7.
$$

Differentiate each term with respect to $x$.

The derivative of $x^2$ is $2x$.

For $xy$, use the product rule:

$$
\dfrac{d}{dx}(xy)=x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y.
$$

The derivative of $y^2$ is

$$
2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}.
$$

So

$$
2x+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0.
$$

Collecting the $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ terms gives

$$
(x+2y)\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-(2x+y).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}
=
-\dfrac{2x+y}{x+2y}.
$$

## 4.7 Parametric differentiation

### Key mathematical ideas

A curve may be defined parametrically by

$$
x=f(t),
\qquad
y=g(t).
$$

If both $x$ and $y$ depend on $t$, then

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}
=
\dfrac{\dfrac{dy}{dt}}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}},
$$

provided

$$
\dfrac{dx}{dt}\neq0.
$$

This is the chain rule in the form

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dt}
=
\dfrac{dy}{dx}\dfrac{dx}{dt}.
$$

### Visualisation using R


### Worked example

A curve is defined by

$$
x=t^2,
\qquad
y=t^3.
$$

Find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

First,

$$
\dfrac{dx}{dt}=2t
$$

and

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dt}=3t^2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}
=
\dfrac{\dfrac{dy}{dt}}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}}
=
\dfrac{3t^2}{2t}.
$$

For $t\neq0$,

$$
\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{3t}{2}.
$$

### Answers to STEP-style extension questions

1. We have
   $$
   f'(x)=nx^{n-1}e^{-x}-x^ne^{-x}
   =
   x^{n-1}e^{-x}(n-x).
   $$
   Since $x>0$, the factors $x^{n-1}$ and $e^{-x}$ are positive. Therefore $f'(x)=0$ only when $x=n$. The derivative changes from positive to negative at $x=n$, so this is a maximum. The maximum value is
   $$
   f(n)=n^ne^{-n}.
   $$

2. For $x>0$,
   $$
   f'(x)=1-\dfrac{1}{x^2}.
   $$
   Thus $f'(x)=0$ when $x=1$. The derivative is negative for $0<x<1$ and positive for $x>1$, so $x=1$ gives a minimum. Therefore,
   $$
   f(x)\geq f(1)=2.
   $$
   Hence
   $$
   x+\dfrac{1}{x}\geq2.
   $$

3. For
   $$
   x^2+xy+y^2=3,
   $$
   implicit differentiation gives
   $$
   \dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{2x+y}{x+2y}.
   $$
   Horizontal tangents occur when $2x+y=0$, so $y=-2x$. Substitution gives $x=\pm1$, producing $(1,-2)$ and $(-1,2)$. Vertical tangents occur when $x+2y=0$, so $x=-2y$. Substitution gives $y=\pm1$, producing $(-2,1)$ and $(2,-1)$.

4. For
   $$
   x=t^2+1,
   \qquad
   y=t^3-3t,
   $$
   we have
   $$
   \dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{3t^2-3}{2t}.
   $$
   Setting this equal to $3$ gives
   $$
   \dfrac{3t^2-3}{2t}=3.
   $$
   Hence
   $$
   t^2-2t-1=0.
   $$
   Therefore,
   $$
   t=1+\sqrt2
   \quad \text{or} \quad
   t=1-\sqrt2.
   $$
   The corresponding points are
   $$
   (4+2\sqrt2,\ 4+2\sqrt2)
   $$
   and
   $$
   (4-2\sqrt2,\ 4-2\sqrt2).
   $$

5. Let
   $$
   f(x)=e^x(a\sin x+b\cos x).
   $$
   Then
   $$
   f'(x)
   =
   e^x(a\sin x+b\cos x)
   +
   e^x(a\cos x-b\sin x).
   $$
   So
   $$
   f'(x)
   =
   e^x\left((a-b)\sin x+(a+b)\cos x\right).
   $$
   We require
   $$
   (a-b)\sin x+(a+b)\cos x=\sin x.
   $$
   Therefore,
   $$
   a-b=1
   $$
   and
   $$
   a+b=0.
   $$
   Solving gives
   $$
   a=\dfrac{1}{2},
   \qquad
   b=-\dfrac{1}{2}.
   $$
