2.2 1.1 The modulus function

2.2.1 Key mathematical ideas

The modulus of a real number is its distance from zero on the number line.

\[ |x| = \begin{cases} x, & x \geq 0,\\ -x, & x < 0. \end{cases} \]

Therefore, \(|x|\) is always non-negative.

For example,

\[ |5|=5,\qquad |-5|=5. \]

A useful equation rule is:

\[ |A|=k,\quad k\geq 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad A=k \ \text{or}\ A=-k. \]

If \(k<0\), then the equation \(|A|=k\) has no real solution.

The sharp corner at the origin occurs because the function changes from \(y=-x\) to \(y=x\).

2.2.3 Worked example

Solve

\[ |3x-2|=10. \]

Since \(|A|=10\) means \(A=10\) or \(A=-10\), we solve

\[ 3x-2=10 \]

or

\[ 3x-2=-10. \]

From the first equation,

\[ 3x=12, \]

so

\[ x=4. \]

From the second equation,

\[ 3x=-8, \]

so

\[ x=-\dfrac{8}{3}. \]

Therefore,

\[ x=4 \quad \text{or} \quad x=-\dfrac{8}{3}. \]

2.3 1.2 Graphs involving modulus functions

2.3.1 Key mathematical ideas

For a linear expression \(ax+b\), the graph

\[ y=|ax+b| \]

has a turning point where

\[ ax+b=0. \]

Solving,

\[ x=-\dfrac{b}{a}. \]

The graph is V-shaped because the output is reflected above the \(x\)-axis.

2.3.2 Visualisation using R

Code 
df <- data.frame(
  x = seq(-2, 5, length.out = 600)
)

ggplot(df, aes(x = x, y = y)) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1.1) +
  geom_hline(yintercept = 0) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 0) +
  geom_vline(xintercept = 3/2, linetype = "dashed") +
  annotate("point", x = 3/2, y = 0, size = 3) +
  annotate("text", x = 3/2, y = 0.8, label = "turning point") +
  labs(
    title = "Graph of y = |2x - 3|",
    subtitle = "The turning point occurs where 2x - 3 = 0.",
    x = "x",
    y = "y"
  ) +

2.3.3 Worked example

Sketch

\[ y=|3x+6|. \]

The expression inside the modulus is zero when

\[ 3x+6=0. \]

Therefore,

\[ x=-2. \]

So the turning point is

\[ (-2,0). \]

For \(x\geq -2\),

\[ |3x+6|=3x+6. \]

For \(x<-2\),

\[ |3x+6|=-(3x+6)=-3x-6. \]

Hence the graph is made from two straight-line pieces meeting at \((-2,0)\).

2.4 1.3 Modulus inequalities

2.4.1 Key mathematical ideas

Modulus inequalities can often be interpreted using distance.

For example,

\[ |x-a|<r \]

means that the distance from \(x\) to \(a\) is less than \(r\).

So

\[ a-r<x<a+r. \]

Similarly,

\[ |x-a|>r \]

means that the distance from \(x\) to \(a\) is greater than \(r\).

So

\[ x<a-r \quad \text{or} \quad x>a+r. \]

2.4.2 Visualisation using R

Code 
df <- data.frame(
  x = seq(-4, 8, length.out = 600)
)

df$y <- abs(df$x - 2)
df$inside <- df$y < 3

ggplot(df, aes(x = x, y = y)) +

    x = "x",

2.4.3 Worked example

Solve

\[ |x-5|\leq 4. \]

This means the distance from \(x\) to \(5\) is at most \(4\).

Therefore,

\[ -4\leq x-5\leq 4. \]

Adding \(5\) throughout gives

\[ 1\leq x\leq 9. \]

So the solution is

\[ x\in[1,9]. \]

2.5 1.4 Polynomial division

2.5.1 Key mathematical ideas

Polynomial division is based on the division algorithm.

If \(P(x)\) is divided by \(D(x)\), then

\[ P(x)=D(x)Q(x)+R(x), \]

where \(Q(x)\) is the quotient and \(R(x)\) is the remainder.

If the divisor is linear, then the remainder is constant.

If the divisor is quadratic, then the remainder is at most linear.

For examination work, the safest written method is long division. Synthetic division is a useful checking method for divisors of the form \(x-a\), but it should not replace the full written method unless it is explicitly allowed.

2.5.2 Worked example using long division

Divide

\[ x^3-2x^2-5x+6 \]

by

\[ x-1. \]

We divide term by term.

First,

\[ \dfrac{x^3}{x}=x^2. \]

Multiply \(x-1\) by \(x^2\):

\[ x^2(x-1)=x^3-x^2. \]

Subtract this from the original polynomial:

\[ \begin{aligned} x^3-2x^2-5x+6-(x^3-x^2) &=x^3-2x^2-5x+6-x^3+x^2\\ &=-x^2-5x+6. \end{aligned} \]

Next,

\[ \dfrac{-x^2}{x}=-x. \]

Multiply \(x-1\) by \(-x\):

\[ -x(x-1)=-x^2+x. \]

Subtract:

\[ \begin{aligned} -x^2-5x+6-(-x^2+x) &=-x^2-5x+6+x^2-x\\ &=-6x+6. \end{aligned} \]

Finally,

\[ \dfrac{-6x}{x}=-6. \]

Multiply \(x-1\) by \(-6\):

\[ -6(x-1)=-6x+6. \]

Subtract:

\[ (-6x+6)-(-6x+6)=0. \]

Therefore,

\[ x^3-2x^2-5x+6=(x-1)(x^2-x-6). \]

So the quotient is

\[ x^2-x-6 \]

and the remainder is

\[ 0. \]

2.5.3 Synthetic division check

Synthetic division gives a quick check when the divisor has the form \(x-a\).

Here, the divisor is \(x-1\), so \(a=1\).

The coefficients of

\[ x^3-2x^2-5x+6 \]

are

\[ 1,\ -2,\ -5,\ 6. \]

The synthetic division check is

\[ \begin{array}{c|rrrr} 1 & 1 & -2 & -5 & 6\\ & & 1 & -1 & -6\\ \hline & 1 & -1 & -6 & 0 \end{array} \]

This gives quotient coefficients

\[ 1,\ -1,\ -6, \]

so the quotient is

\[ x^2-x-6. \]

The final number is \(0\), so the remainder is \(0\).

This agrees with the long division result.

Warning

Synthetic division is included here as a checking method. For examination working, present the long division method unless the examination explicitly allows synthetic division.

2.5.6 Synthetic division in R

The following code performs the synthetic division check.

Code 
root <- 1
coefficients <- c(1, -2, -5, 6)

bottom_row <- numeric(length(coefficients))
bottom_row[1] <- coefficients[1]

for (i in 2:length(coefficients)) {
  bottom_row[i] <- coefficients[i] + root * bottom_row[i - 1]
}

data.frame(
  coefficient = coefficients,
  synthetic_bottom_row = bottom_row
)
  coefficient synthetic_bottom_row
1           1                    1
2          -2                   -1
3          -5                   -6
4           6                    0

The last entry is the remainder. The previous entries are the quotient coefficients.

2.6 1.5 The factor theorem

2.6.1 Key mathematical ideas

The factor theorem states:

If

\[ P(a)=0, \]

then

\[ x-a \]

is a factor of \(P(x)\).

Conversely, if \(x-a\) is a factor of \(P(x)\), then

\[ P(a)=0. \]

2.6.2 Worked example

Show that \(x-2\) is a factor of

\[ P(x)=x^3-5x^2+2x+8. \]

By the factor theorem, \(x-2\) is a factor if \(P(2)=0\).

Now,

\[ P(2)=2^3-5(2)^2+2(2)+8. \]

So

\[ P(2)=8-20+4+8=0. \]

Therefore,

\[ x-2 \]

is a factor of \(P(x)\).

To factorise fully, divide \(P(x)\) by \(x-2\).

Using long division,

\[ x^3-5x^2+2x+8=(x-2)(x^2-3x-4). \]

Then

\[ x^2-3x-4=(x-4)(x+1). \]

Therefore,

\[ P(x)=(x-2)(x-4)(x+1). \]

2.6.3 Synthetic division check

For the divisor \(x-2\), we use \(a=2\).

The coefficients of

\[ x^3-5x^2+2x+8 \]

are

\[ 1,\ -5,\ 2,\ 8. \]

The synthetic division check is

\[ \begin{array}{c|rrrr} 2 & 1 & -5 & 2 & 8\\ & & 2 & -6 & -8\\ \hline & 1 & -3 & -4 & 0 \end{array} \]

This confirms that the quotient is

\[ x^2-3x-4 \]

and the remainder is \(0\).

2.7 1.6 The remainder theorem

2.7.1 Key mathematical ideas

The remainder theorem states:

When \(P(x)\) is divided by \(x-a\), the remainder is

\[ P(a). \]

More generally, when \(P(x)\) is divided by \(ax-b\), the remainder is

\[ P\left(\dfrac{b}{a}\right). \]

This is connected to polynomial division because if

\[ P(x)=(x-a)Q(x)+R, \]

then substituting \(x=a\) gives

\[ P(a)=(a-a)Q(a)+R. \]

Therefore,

\[ P(a)=R. \]

2.7.2 Worked example

Find the remainder when

\[ P(x)=x^3-2x^2-5x+6 \]

is divided by \(x-2\).

By the remainder theorem, the remainder is \(P(2)\).

Now,

\[ P(2)=2^3-2(2)^2-5(2)+6. \]

So

\[ P(2)=8-8-10+6=-4. \]

Therefore, the remainder is

\[ -4. \]

2.7.3 Long division check

Dividing

\[ x^3-2x^2-5x+6 \]

by

\[ x-2 \]

using long division gives:

First,

\[ \dfrac{x^3}{x}=x^2. \]

Subtract

\[ x^2(x-2)=x^3-2x^2. \]

Then

\[ x^3-2x^2-5x+6-(x^3-2x^2)=-5x+6. \]

Next,

\[ \dfrac{-5x}{x}=-5. \]

Subtract

\[ -5(x-2)=-5x+10. \]

Then

\[ (-5x+6)-(-5x+10)=-4. \]

Therefore,

\[ x^3-2x^2-5x+6=(x-2)(x^2-5)-4. \]

The remainder is again

\[ -4. \]

2.7.4 Synthetic division check

For the divisor \(x-2\), we use \(a=2\).

The coefficients are

\[ 1,\ -2,\ -5,\ 6. \]

The synthetic division check is

\[ \begin{array}{c|rrrr} 2 & 1 & -2 & -5 & 6\\ & & 2 & 0 & -10\\ \hline & 1 & 0 & -5 & -4 \end{array} \]

This gives quotient

\[ x^2-5 \]

and remainder

\[ -4. \]

## 1.1 The modulus function

### Key mathematical ideas

The modulus of a real number is its distance from zero on the number line.

$$
|x| =
\begin{cases}
x, & x \geq 0,\\
-x, & x < 0.
\end{cases}
$$

Therefore, $|x|$ is always non-negative.

For example,

$$
|5|=5,\qquad |-5|=5.
$$

A useful equation rule is:

$$
|A|=k,\quad k\geq 0
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
A=k \ \text{or}\ A=-k.
$$

If $k<0$, then the equation $|A|=k$ has no real solution.

### Visualisation using R

The sharp corner at the origin occurs because the function changes from $y=-x$ to $y=x$.

### Worked example

Solve

$$
|3x-2|=10.
$$

Since $|A|=10$ means $A=10$ or $A=-10$, we solve

$$
3x-2=10
$$

or

$$
3x-2=-10.
$$

From the first equation,

$$
3x=12,
$$

so

$$
x=4.
$$

From the second equation,

$$
3x=-8,
$$

so

$$
x=-\dfrac{8}{3}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
x=4
\quad \text{or} \quad
x=-\dfrac{8}{3}.
$$

## 1.2 Graphs involving modulus functions

### Key mathematical ideas

For a linear expression $ax+b$, the graph

$$
y=|ax+b|
$$

has a turning point where

$$
ax+b=0.
$$

Solving,

$$
x=-\dfrac{b}{a}.
$$

The graph is V-shaped because the output is reflected above the $x$-axis.

## 1.3 Modulus inequalities

### Key mathematical ideas

Modulus inequalities can often be interpreted using distance.

For example,

$$
|x-a|<r
$$

means that the distance from $x$ to $a$ is less than $r$.

So

$$
a-r<x<a+r.
$$

Similarly,

$$
|x-a|>r
$$

means that the distance from $x$ to $a$ is greater than $r$.

So

$$
x<a-r
\quad \text{or} \quad
x>a+r.
$$

### Visualisation using R


### Worked example

Solve

$$
|x-5|\leq 4.
$$

This means the distance from $x$ to $5$ is at most $4$.

Therefore,

$$
-4\leq x-5\leq 4.
$$

Adding $5$ throughout gives

$$
1\leq x\leq 9.
$$

So the solution is

$$
x\in[1,9].
$$

## 1.4 Polynomial division

### Key mathematical ideas

Polynomial division is based on the division algorithm.

If $P(x)$ is divided by $D(x)$, then

$$
P(x)=D(x)Q(x)+R(x),
$$

where $Q(x)$ is the quotient and $R(x)$ is the remainder.

If the divisor is linear, then the remainder is constant.

If the divisor is quadratic, then the remainder is at most linear.

For examination work, the safest written method is **long division**. Synthetic division is a useful checking method for divisors of the form $x-a$, but it should not replace the full written method unless it is explicitly allowed.

### Worked example using long division

Divide

$$
x^3-2x^2-5x+6
$$

by

$$
x-1.
$$

We divide term by term.

First,

$$
\dfrac{x^3}{x}=x^2.
$$

Multiply $x-1$ by $x^2$:

$$
x^2(x-1)=x^3-x^2.
$$

Subtract this from the original polynomial:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x^3-2x^2-5x+6-(x^3-x^2)
&=x^3-2x^2-5x+6-x^3+x^2\\
&=-x^2-5x+6.
\end{aligned}
$$

Next,

$$
\dfrac{-x^2}{x}=-x.
$$

Multiply $x-1$ by $-x$:

$$
-x(x-1)=-x^2+x.
$$

Subtract:

$$
\begin{aligned}
-x^2-5x+6-(-x^2+x)
&=-x^2-5x+6+x^2-x\\
&=-6x+6.
\end{aligned}
$$

Finally,

$$
\dfrac{-6x}{x}=-6.
$$

Multiply $x-1$ by $-6$:

$$
-6(x-1)=-6x+6.
$$

Subtract:

$$
(-6x+6)-(-6x+6)=0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
x^3-2x^2-5x+6=(x-1)(x^2-x-6).
$$

So the quotient is

$$
x^2-x-6
$$

and the remainder is

$$
0.
$$

### Synthetic division check

Synthetic division gives a quick check when the divisor has the form $x-a$.

Here, the divisor is $x-1$, so $a=1$.

The coefficients of

$$
x^3-2x^2-5x+6
$$

are

$$
1,\ -2,\ -5,\ 6.
$$

The synthetic division check is

$$
\begin{array}{c|rrrr}
1 & 1 & -2 & -5 & 6\\
  &   & 1 & -1 & -6\\
\hline
  & 1 & -1 & -6 & 0
\end{array}
$$

This gives quotient coefficients

$$
1,\ -1,\ -6,
$$

so the quotient is

$$
x^2-x-6.
$$

The final number is $0$, so the remainder is $0$.

This agrees with the long division result.

::: {.callout-warning}
Synthetic division is included here as a checking method. For examination working, present the long division method unless the examination explicitly allows synthetic division.
:::

## 1.5 The factor theorem

### Key mathematical ideas

The factor theorem states:

If

$$
P(a)=0,
$$

then

$$
x-a
$$

is a factor of $P(x)$.

Conversely, if $x-a$ is a factor of $P(x)$, then

$$
P(a)=0.
$$

### Worked example

Show that $x-2$ is a factor of

$$
P(x)=x^3-5x^2+2x+8.
$$

By the factor theorem, $x-2$ is a factor if $P(2)=0$.

Now,

$$
P(2)=2^3-5(2)^2+2(2)+8.
$$

So

$$
P(2)=8-20+4+8=0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
x-2
$$

is a factor of $P(x)$.

To factorise fully, divide $P(x)$ by $x-2$.

Using long division,

$$
x^3-5x^2+2x+8=(x-2)(x^2-3x-4).
$$

Then

$$
x^2-3x-4=(x-4)(x+1).
$$

Therefore,

$$
P(x)=(x-2)(x-4)(x+1).
$$

### Synthetic division check

For the divisor $x-2$, we use $a=2$.

The coefficients of

$$
x^3-5x^2+2x+8
$$

are

$$
1,\ -5,\ 2,\ 8.
$$

The synthetic division check is

$$
\begin{array}{c|rrrr}
2 & 1 & -5 & 2 & 8\\
  &   & 2 & -6 & -8\\
\hline
  & 1 & -3 & -4 & 0
\end{array}
$$

This confirms that the quotient is

$$
x^2-3x-4
$$

and the remainder is $0$.

## 1.6 The remainder theorem

### Key mathematical ideas

The remainder theorem states:

When $P(x)$ is divided by $x-a$, the remainder is

$$
P(a).
$$

More generally, when $P(x)$ is divided by $ax-b$, the remainder is

$$
P\left(\dfrac{b}{a}\right).
$$

This is connected to polynomial division because if

$$
P(x)=(x-a)Q(x)+R,
$$

then substituting $x=a$ gives

$$
P(a)=(a-a)Q(a)+R.
$$

Therefore,

$$
P(a)=R.
$$

### Worked example

Find the remainder when

$$
P(x)=x^3-2x^2-5x+6
$$

is divided by $x-2$.

By the remainder theorem, the remainder is $P(2)$.

Now,

$$
P(2)=2^3-2(2)^2-5(2)+6.
$$

So

$$
P(2)=8-8-10+6=-4.
$$

Therefore, the remainder is

$$
-4.
$$

### Long division check

Dividing

$$
x^3-2x^2-5x+6
$$

by

$$
x-2
$$

using long division gives:

First,

$$
\dfrac{x^3}{x}=x^2.
$$

Subtract

$$
x^2(x-2)=x^3-2x^2.
$$

Then

$$
x^3-2x^2-5x+6-(x^3-2x^2)=-5x+6.
$$

Next,

$$
\dfrac{-5x}{x}=-5.
$$

Subtract

$$
-5(x-2)=-5x+10.
$$

Then

$$
(-5x+6)-(-5x+10)=-4.
$$

Therefore,

$$
x^3-2x^2-5x+6=(x-2)(x^2-5)-4.
$$

The remainder is again

$$
-4.
$$

### Synthetic division check

For the divisor $x-2$, we use $a=2$.

The coefficients are

$$
1,\ -2,\ -5,\ 6.
$$

The synthetic division check is

$$
\begin{array}{c|rrrr}
2 & 1 & -2 & -5 & 6\\
  &   & 2 & 0 & -10\\
\hline
  & 1 & 0 & -5 & -4
\end{array}
$$

This gives quotient

$$
x^2-5
$$

and remainder

$$
-4.
$$
