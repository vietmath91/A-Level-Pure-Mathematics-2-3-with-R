7.1 Overview

This chapter develops three connected numerical methods ideas:

  1. finding a starting point for a root;
  2. improving a solution using iteration;
  3. using iterative processes to solve problems involving other areas of mathematics.

The central aim is to understand how equations can be solved approximately when exact algebraic methods are difficult or unavailable.

Note

The exercises are organised into tiers.

Bronze develops fluency.
Silver develops simple application.
Gold develops exam-style reasoning.
The STEP-style section extends the chapter beyond routine A Level fluency.

7.2 6.1 Finding a starting point

7.2.1 Key mathematical ideas

A root of the equation

\[ f(x)=0 \]

is a value of \(x\) where the graph of \(y=f(x)\) crosses or touches the \(x\)-axis.

A common way to locate a starting interval is the change of sign test.

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and

\[ f(a)f(b)<0, \]

then there is at least one root of

\[ f(x)=0 \]

between \(a\) and \(b\).

This works because a continuous graph cannot move from one side of the \(x\)-axis to the other without crossing it.

However, the test must be used carefully:

  • a change of sign guarantees at least one root only when the function is continuous;
  • no change of sign does not always mean there is no root;
  • a root may touch the axis without crossing it.

7.2.3 Worked example

Show that the equation

\[ x^3-x-4=0 \]

has a root between \(1\) and \(2\).

Let

\[ f(x)=x^3-x-4. \]

Then

\[ f(1)=1^3-1-4=-4. \]

Also,

\[ f(2)=2^3-2-4=2. \]

Since

\[ f(1)<0 \]

and

\[ f(2)>0, \]

there is a change of sign between \(1\) and \(2\).

Therefore, since \(f\) is continuous, the equation

\[ x^3-x-4=0 \]

has at least one root between \(1\) and \(2\).

7.3 6.2 Improving your solution

7.3.1 Key mathematical ideas

Once we have a starting point or a starting interval, we can improve the approximation using an iterative formula.

An iterative formula has the form

\[ x_{n+1}=g(x_n). \]

This means:

  • start with an initial value \(x_1\);
  • substitute it into the formula to get \(x_2\);
  • substitute \(x_2\) into the formula to get \(x_3\);
  • continue until the values appear stable to the required accuracy.

If the sequence converges to a value \(\alpha\), then

\[ \alpha=g(\alpha). \]

So \(\alpha\) is a fixed point of \(g\).

An iteration may:

  • converge quickly;
  • converge slowly;
  • oscillate while converging;
  • enter a periodic loop;
  • diverge.

A starting point and a rearrangement must therefore be chosen carefully.

7.3.3 Worked example

The equation

\[ x^3-x-4=0 \]

can be rearranged as

\[ x=\sqrt[3]{x+4}. \]

This gives the iterative formula

\[ x_{n+1}=\sqrt[3]{x_n+4}. \]

Starting with

\[ x_1=1.5, \]

we obtain

\[ x_2=\sqrt[3]{1.5+4}=1.765174\ldots \]

Then

\[ x_3=\sqrt[3]{1.765174\ldots+4}=1.793098\ldots \]

and

\[ x_4=1.795988\ldots \]

Continuing,

\[ x_5=1.796287\ldots \]

and

\[ x_6=1.796318\ldots \]

The values are settling down to

\[ x\approx1.796. \]

Therefore, the root of

\[ x^3-x-4=0 \]

is approximately

\[ 1.796 \]

to three decimal places.

7.3.4 Why starting point and rearrangement matter

The same equation

\[ x^3-x-4=0 \]

can also be rearranged as

\[ x=x^3-4. \]

This gives

\[ x_{n+1}=x_n^3-4. \]

Starting from \(x_1=1.5\) gives

\[ x_2=-0.625, \]

\[ x_3=-4.244140625, \]

and then the values move rapidly away from the root.

So the rearrangement

\[ x_{n+1}=x_n^3-4 \]

is a poor iteration for this root.

7.3.7 A useful convergence test

Suppose the iteration is

\[ x_{n+1}=g(x_n) \]

and \(\alpha\) is a fixed point.

If

\[ |g'(\alpha)|<1, \]

then the iteration is usually locally convergent near \(\alpha\).

If

\[ |g'(\alpha)|>1, \]

then the iteration is usually locally divergent near \(\alpha\).

For

\[ g(x)=\sqrt[3]{x+4}, \]

we have

\[ g'(x)=\dfrac{1}{3(x+4)^{2/3}}. \]

Near the root \(\alpha\approx1.796\),

\[ |g'(\alpha)|\approx0.103<1, \]

so the iteration converges.

For

\[ g(x)=x^3-4, \]

we have

\[ g'(x)=3x^2. \]

Near \(\alpha\approx1.796\),

\[ |g'(\alpha)|\approx9.68>1, \]

so the iteration diverges.

7.4 6.3 Using iterative processes to solve problems involving other areas of mathematics

7.4.1 Key mathematical ideas

Iteration is not restricted to polynomial equations. It can be used in many areas of mathematics, including:

  • exponential equations;
  • logarithmic equations;
  • trigonometric equations;
  • geometry problems leading to transcendental equations;
  • equations where exact algebraic methods are unavailable.

For example, the equation

\[ \cos x=x \]

cannot be solved exactly using elementary algebra.

But it can be written as

\[ x=\cos x, \]

which gives the iteration

\[ x_{n+1}=\cos x_n. \]

The solution is the fixed point where the graphs of

\[ y=\cos x \]

and

\[ y=x \]

intersect.

7.4.3 Worked example

Use iteration to solve

\[ \cos x=x \]

to three decimal places.

Use

\[ x_{n+1}=\cos x_n \]

with

\[ x_1=0.5. \]

Then

\[ x_2=\cos(0.5)=0.877582\ldots \]

\[ x_3=\cos(0.877582\ldots)=0.639012\ldots \]

\[ x_4=0.802685\ldots \]

The values oscillate but slowly converge.

Continuing gives

\[ x\approx0.739. \]

Therefore, the solution of

\[ \cos x=x \]

is

\[ x=0.739 \]

to three decimal places.

7.4.4 A trigonometric example

The equation

\[ \theta=2\sin\theta \]

has the fixed-point iteration

\[ \theta_{n+1}=2\sin\theta_n. \]

This type of equation can occur when a geometric or circular problem produces both a linear angle term and a trigonometric term.

7.4.5 Worked example

Use the iteration

\[ \theta_{n+1}=2\sin\theta_n \]

with

\[ \theta_1=1.85 \]

to solve

\[ \theta=2\sin\theta \]

to three decimal places.

The first few iterations are

\[ \theta_2=1.922550\ldots, \]

\[ \theta_3=1.877539\ldots, \]

\[ \theta_4=1.906644\ldots, \]

\[ \theta_5=1.888262\ldots. \]

The sequence oscillates while converging.

Continuing gives

\[ \theta\approx1.895. \]

To confirm the rounding, define

\[ f(\theta)=\theta-2\sin\theta. \]

Then

\[ f(1.8945)<0 \]

and

\[ f(1.8955)>0. \]

Therefore, the non-zero solution is

\[ \theta=1.895 \]

to three decimal places.

7.5 STEP-style extension questions

These questions are designed to require proof, structure or exact reasoning.

7.5.1 STEP-style Question 1

Let \(f\) be continuous on \([a,b]\).

Suppose

\[ f(a)<0 \]

and

\[ f(b)>0. \]

Explain why there must be at least one value \(\alpha\in(a,b)\) such that

\[ f(\alpha)=0. \]

Then give an example showing why continuity is needed.

7.5.2 STEP-style Question 2

Suppose \(g\) maps the interval \([a,b]\) into itself and there is a constant \(k\) such that

\[ 0<k<1 \]

and

\[ |g'(x)|\leq k \]

for every \(x\in[a,b]\).

Let \(\alpha\) be a fixed point of \(g\) in \([a,b]\).

Show that the iteration

\[ x_{n+1}=g(x_n) \]

satisfies

\[ |x_{n+1}-\alpha|\leq k|x_n-\alpha|. \]

Hence explain why the iteration converges to \(\alpha\).

7.5.3 STEP-style Question 3

The equation

\[ x^2=2 \]

can be written as

\[ x=\dfrac{2}{x}. \]

This gives the iteration

\[ x_{n+1}=\dfrac{2}{x_n}. \]

Show that if \(x_1\neq\sqrt2\) and \(x_1\neq-\sqrt2\), then the sequence does not converge but enters a period-two loop.

7.5.4 STEP-style Question 4

The equation

\[ x^3-x-4=0 \]

can be rearranged in two ways:

\[ x=\sqrt[3]{x+4} \]

and

\[ x=x^3-4. \]

Let \(\alpha\) be the root between \(1\) and \(2\).

Use derivatives of the two iteration functions to explain why

\[ x_{n+1}=\sqrt[3]{x_n+4} \]

converges near \(\alpha\), while

\[ x_{n+1}=x_n^3-4 \]

diverges near \(\alpha\).

7.5.5 STEP-style Question 5

Let

\[ f(x)=\cos x-x. \]

Use calculus and a change of sign argument to prove that the equation

\[ \cos x=x \]

has exactly one solution in \([0,1]\).

Then explain why the iteration

\[ x_{n+1}=\cos x_n \]

converges with oscillation when started near the solution.

## 6.1 Finding a starting point

### Key mathematical ideas

A root of the equation

$$
f(x)=0
$$

is a value of $x$ where the graph of $y=f(x)$ crosses or touches the $x$-axis.

A common way to locate a starting interval is the **change of sign test**.

If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and

$$
f(a)f(b)<0,
$$

then there is at least one root of

$$
f(x)=0
$$

between $a$ and $b$.

This works because a continuous graph cannot move from one side of the $x$-axis to the other without crossing it.

However, the test must be used carefully:

- a change of sign guarantees at least one root only when the function is continuous;
- no change of sign does not always mean there is no root;
- a root may touch the axis without crossing it.

## 6.2 Improving your solution

### Key mathematical ideas

Once we have a starting point or a starting interval, we can improve the approximation using an **iterative formula**.

An iterative formula has the form

$$
x_{n+1}=g(x_n).
$$

This means:

- start with an initial value $x_1$;
- substitute it into the formula to get $x_2$;
- substitute $x_2$ into the formula to get $x_3$;
- continue until the values appear stable to the required accuracy.

If the sequence converges to a value $\alpha$, then

$$
\alpha=g(\alpha).
$$

So $\alpha$ is a fixed point of $g$.

An iteration may:

- converge quickly;
- converge slowly;
- oscillate while converging;
- enter a periodic loop;
- diverge.

A starting point and a rearrangement must therefore be chosen carefully.

### A useful convergence test

Suppose the iteration is

$$
x_{n+1}=g(x_n)
$$

and $\alpha$ is a fixed point.

If

$$
|g'(\alpha)|<1,
$$

then the iteration is usually locally convergent near $\alpha$.

If

$$
|g'(\alpha)|>1,
$$

then the iteration is usually locally divergent near $\alpha$.

For

$$
g(x)=\sqrt[3]{x+4},
$$

we have

$$
g'(x)=\dfrac{1}{3(x+4)^{2/3}}.
$$

Near the root $\alpha\approx1.796$,

$$
|g'(\alpha)|\approx0.103<1,
$$

so the iteration converges.

For

$$
g(x)=x^3-4,
$$

we have

$$
g'(x)=3x^2.
$$

Near $\alpha\approx1.796$,

$$
|g'(\alpha)|\approx9.68>1,
$$

so the iteration diverges.

## 6.3 Using iterative processes to solve problems involving other areas of mathematics

### Key mathematical ideas

Iteration is not restricted to polynomial equations. It can be used in many areas of mathematics, including:

- exponential equations;
- logarithmic equations;
- trigonometric equations;
- geometry problems leading to transcendental equations;
- equations where exact algebraic methods are unavailable.

For example, the equation

$$
\cos x=x
$$

cannot be solved exactly using elementary algebra.

But it can be written as

$$
x=\cos x,
$$

which gives the iteration

$$
x_{n+1}=\cos x_n.
$$

The solution is the fixed point where the graphs of

$$
y=\cos x
$$

and

$$
y=x
$$

intersect.

### Answers to STEP-style extension questions

1. Since $f$ is continuous, its graph cannot move from a negative value at $x=a$ to a positive value at $x=b$ without crossing the $x$-axis. Therefore there must be some $\alpha\in(a,b)$ such that
   $$
   f(\alpha)=0.
   $$
   Continuity is needed. For example,
   $$
   f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x-1}
   $$
   changes sign between $0$ and $2$, but has no root in that interval because it is discontinuous at $x=1$.

2. By the mean value theorem, for some point $c$ between $x_n$ and $\alpha$,
   $$
   g(x_n)-g(\alpha)=g'(c)(x_n-\alpha).
   $$
   Since $g(\alpha)=\alpha$,
   $$
   x_{n+1}-\alpha=g'(c)(x_n-\alpha).
   $$
   Taking absolute values gives
   $$
   |x_{n+1}-\alpha|
   =
   |g'(c)||x_n-\alpha|
   \leq
   k|x_n-\alpha|.
   $$
   Since $0<k<1$, the error shrinks at each step, so the iteration converges to $\alpha$.

3. The iteration is
   $$
   x_{n+1}=\dfrac{2}{x_n}.
   $$
   Then
   $$
   x_{n+2}=\dfrac{2}{x_{n+1}}
   =
   \dfrac{2}{\dfrac{2}{x_n}}
   =
   x_n.
   $$
   Hence the sequence repeats every two terms. It is constant only when
   $$
   x_n=\dfrac{2}{x_n},
   $$
   giving
   $$
   x_n^2=2.
   $$
   Therefore the sequence converges only if the starting value is already $\sqrt2$ or $-\sqrt2$.

4. Let
   $$
   g_1(x)=\sqrt[3]{x+4}.
   $$
   Then
   $$
   g_1'(x)=\dfrac{1}{3(x+4)^{2/3}}.
   $$
   At the root $\alpha\approx1.796$,
   $$
   |g_1'(\alpha)|\approx0.103<1.
   $$
   So the iteration
   $$
   x_{n+1}=\sqrt[3]{x_n+4}
   $$
   converges near $\alpha$.

   Now let
   $$
   g_2(x)=x^3-4.
   $$
   Then
   $$
   g_2'(x)=3x^2.
   $$
   At $\alpha\approx1.796$,
   $$
   |g_2'(\alpha)|\approx9.68>1.
   $$
   Therefore the iteration
   $$
   x_{n+1}=x_n^3-4
   $$
   diverges near $\alpha$.

5. Let
   $$
   f(x)=\cos x-x.
   $$
   Then
   $$
   f(0)=1>0
   $$
   and
   $$
   f(1)=\cos1-1<0.
   $$
   So there is at least one root in $(0,1)$.

   Also,
   $$
   f'(x)=-\sin x-1<0
   $$
   for $0\leq x\leq1$.
   Therefore $f$ is strictly decreasing and can cross the $x$-axis only once. Hence the solution is unique.

   The iteration
   $$
   x_{n+1}=\cos x_n
   $$
   has iteration function $g(x)=\cos x$. At the fixed point $\alpha$,
   $$
   g'(\alpha)=-\sin\alpha.
   $$
   Since $0<\sin\alpha<1$, we have
   $$
   |g'(\alpha)|<1.
   $$
   Therefore the iteration converges near $\alpha$. Since $g'(\alpha)<0$, the convergence is oscillatory.
