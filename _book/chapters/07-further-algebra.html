8.1 Overview

This chapter develops five connected algebraic ideas:

  1. improper algebraic fractions;
  2. partial fractions;
  3. binomial expansion of \((1+x)^n\) for values of \(n\) that are not positive integers;
  4. binomial expansion of \((a+x)^n\) for values of \(n\) that are not positive integers;
  5. using partial fractions together with binomial expansions.

The central aim is to rewrite algebraic expressions into forms that are easier to expand, approximate, integrate or sum.

Note

The exercises are organised into tiers.

Bronze develops fluency.
Silver develops simple application.
Gold develops exam-style reasoning.
The STEP-style section extends the chapter beyond routine A Level fluency.

8.2 7.1 Improper algebraic fractions

8.2.1 Key mathematical ideas

An algebraic fraction is proper when

\[ \deg(\text{numerator})<\deg(\text{denominator}). \]

It is improper when

\[ \deg(\text{numerator})\geq\deg(\text{denominator}). \]

If an algebraic fraction is improper, do long division first.

The division algorithm says that

\[ P(x)=D(x)Q(x)+R(x), \]

where \(Q(x)\) is the quotient and \(R(x)\) is the remainder.

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{P(x)}{D(x)} = Q(x)+\dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}. \]

The fraction

\[ \dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)} \]

is then proper.

Warning

Before using partial fractions, always check the degrees. If the power of the top is greater than or equal to the power of the bottom, do long division first.

8.2.3 Worked example

Express

\[ \dfrac{2x^3+3x^2-x+5}{x^2+1} \]

as the sum of a polynomial and a proper fraction.

Since the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator, we divide first.

First,

\[ \dfrac{2x^3}{x^2}=2x. \]

Multiply:

\[ 2x(x^2+1)=2x^3+2x. \]

Subtract:

\[ 2x^3+3x^2-x+5-(2x^3+2x)=3x^2-3x+5. \]

Next,

\[ \dfrac{3x^2}{x^2}=3. \]

Multiply:

\[ 3(x^2+1)=3x^2+3. \]

Subtract:

\[ 3x^2-3x+5-(3x^2+3)=-3x+2. \]

Therefore,

\[ 2x^3+3x^2-x+5 = (x^2+1)(2x+3)+(-3x+2). \]

Hence,

\[ \dfrac{2x^3+3x^2-x+5}{x^2+1} = 2x+3+\dfrac{-3x+2}{x^2+1}. \]

8.3 7.2 Partial fractions

8.3.1 Key mathematical ideas

Partial fractions reverse the process of adding algebraic fractions.

This chapter uses three main proper-fraction types.

8.3.2 Type 1: distinct linear factors

If the denominator has distinct linear factors, use one constant numerator over each factor.

For example,

\[ \dfrac{5x+1}{(x-1)(x+2)} = \dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{B}{x+2}. \]

8.3.3 Type 2: repeated linear factors

If the denominator has a repeated linear factor, include each power of that factor.

For example,

\[ \dfrac{3x+5}{(x-1)^2} = \dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{B}{(x-1)^2}. \]

For a denominator such as \((x-1)^3\), we would use

\[ \dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{B}{(x-1)^2}+\dfrac{C}{(x-1)^3}. \]

8.3.4 Type 3: an irreducible quadratic factor

If the denominator contains a quadratic factor that cannot be factorised over the real numbers, use a linear numerator over that quadratic.

For example,

\[ \dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)} = \dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+2}. \]

8.3.5 Worked example

Express

\[ \dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)} \]

in partial fractions.

Write

\[ \dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)} = \dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+2}. \]

Multiplying throughout by \((x-1)(x^2+2)\) gives

\[ 7x-1=A(x^2+2)+(Bx+C)(x-1). \]

Expanding,

\[ 7x-1 = A x^2+2A+Bx^2-Bx+Cx-C. \]

Collecting terms,

\[ 7x-1 = (A+B)x^2+(C-B)x+(2A-C). \]

Equating coefficients gives

\[ A+B=0, \]

\[ C-B=7, \]

and

\[ 2A-C=-1. \]

Solving gives

\[ A=2,\quad B=-2,\quad C=5. \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)} = \dfrac{2}{x-1}+\dfrac{-2x+5}{x^2+2}. \]

8.4 7.3 Binomial expansion of \((1+x)^n\) for values of \(n\) that are not positive integers

8.4.1 Key mathematical ideas

For any rational number \(n\),

\[ (1+x)^n = 1+nx+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 +\cdots \]

provided

\[ |x|<1. \]

When \(n\) is not a non-negative integer, the expansion does not usually terminate. The more terms we include, the better the approximation tends to be inside the interval of validity.

8.4.2 Visualisation using R

Code 
power <- -0.5

x_values <- seq(-0.9, 0.9, length.out = 800)

exact_df <- data.frame(

approx_df <- expand.grid(
  x = x_values,

approx_df$terms_label <- paste0("up to x^", approx_df$highest_power)
approx_df$y_value <- mapply(
  function(x_input, highest_power_input) {

8.4.3 Worked example

Expand

\[ (1+x)^{-2} \]

up to and including the term in \(x^3\).

Here,

\[ n=-2. \]

So

\[ (1+x)^{-2} = 1+(-2)x + \dfrac{(-2)(-3)}{2!}x^2 + \dfrac{(-2)(-3)(-4)}{3!}x^3 +\cdots \]

Therefore,

\[ (1+x)^{-2} = 1-2x+3x^2-4x^3+\cdots. \]

The expansion is valid for

\[ |x|<1. \]

8.5 7.4 Binomial expansion of \((a+x)^n\) for values of \(n\) that are not positive integers

8.5.1 Key mathematical ideas

To expand

\[ (a+x)^n, \]

factor out \(a\) first:

\[ (a+x)^n = a^n\left(1+\dfrac{x}{a}\right)^n. \]

Then apply the binomial expansion to

\[ \left(1+\dfrac{x}{a}\right)^n. \]

The expansion is valid when

\[ \left|\dfrac{x}{a}\right|<1. \]

Equivalently,

\[ |x|<|a|. \]

8.5.3 Worked example

Expand

\[ (4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}} \]

up to and including the term in \(x^3\).

First write

\[ (4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 4^{\frac{1}{2}}\left(1+\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}. \]

So

\[ (4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 2\left(1+\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}. \]

Now

\[ (1+u)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 1+\dfrac{1}{2}u-\dfrac{1}{8}u^2+\dfrac{1}{16}u^3+\cdots. \]

Using

\[ u=\dfrac{x}{4}, \]

we get

\[ (4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 2\left[ 1+\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{x}{4}\right) -\dfrac{1}{8}\left(\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^2 +\dfrac{1}{16}\left(\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^3 +\cdots \right]. \]

Therefore,

\[ (4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}} = 2+\dfrac{x}{4}-\dfrac{x^2}{64}+\dfrac{x^3}{512}+\cdots. \]

The expansion is valid when

\[ |x|<4. \]

8.6 7.5 Partial fractions and binomial expansions

8.6.1 Key mathematical ideas

Partial fractions are often used before binomial expansion.

For example,

\[ \dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)} \]

can be split into simpler fractions:

\[ \dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)} = \dfrac{2}{1-x}-\dfrac{1}{1+2x}. \]

Each term can then be expanded separately.

The expansion

\[ \dfrac{1}{1-x} = 1+x+x^2+x^3+\cdots \]

is valid for

\[ |x|<1. \]

The expansion

\[ \dfrac{1}{1+2x} = 1-2x+4x^2-8x^3+\cdots \]

is valid for

\[ |2x|<1. \]

So the combined expansion is valid for

\[ |x|<\dfrac{1}{2}. \]

8.6.3 Worked example

Obtain the expansion of

\[ \dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)} \]

in ascending powers of \(x\), up to and including the term in \(x^3\).

First write

\[ \dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)} = \dfrac{A}{1-x}+\dfrac{B}{1+2x}. \]

Multiplying through by \((1-x)(1+2x)\) gives

\[ 1+5x=A(1+2x)+B(1-x). \]

Equating coefficients gives

\[ A+B=1 \]

and

\[ 2A-B=5. \]

Solving gives

\[ A=2,\quad B=-1. \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)} = \dfrac{2}{1-x}-\dfrac{1}{1+2x}. \]

Now,

\[ \dfrac{2}{1-x} = 2(1+x+x^2+x^3+\cdots) \]

and

\[ -\dfrac{1}{1+2x} = -(1-2x+4x^2-8x^3+\cdots). \]

Therefore,

\[ \dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)} = 1+4x-2x^2+10x^3+\cdots. \]

The expansion is valid when

\[ |x|<\dfrac{1}{2}. \]

## 7.1 Improper algebraic fractions

### Key mathematical ideas

An algebraic fraction is **proper** when

$$
\deg(\text{numerator})<\deg(\text{denominator}).
$$

It is **improper** when

$$
\deg(\text{numerator})\geq\deg(\text{denominator}).
$$

If an algebraic fraction is improper, do long division first.

The division algorithm says that

$$
P(x)=D(x)Q(x)+R(x),
$$

where $Q(x)$ is the quotient and $R(x)$ is the remainder.

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{P(x)}{D(x)}
=
Q(x)+\dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}.
$$

The fraction

$$
\dfrac{R(x)}{D(x)}
$$

is then proper.

::: {.callout-warning}
Before using partial fractions, always check the degrees. If the power of the top is greater than or equal to the power of the bottom, do long division first.
:::

### Worked example

Express

$$
\dfrac{2x^3+3x^2-x+5}{x^2+1}
$$

as the sum of a polynomial and a proper fraction.

Since the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator, we divide first.

First,

$$
\dfrac{2x^3}{x^2}=2x.
$$

Multiply:

$$
2x(x^2+1)=2x^3+2x.
$$

Subtract:

$$
2x^3+3x^2-x+5-(2x^3+2x)=3x^2-3x+5.
$$

Next,

$$
\dfrac{3x^2}{x^2}=3.
$$

Multiply:

$$
3(x^2+1)=3x^2+3.
$$

Subtract:

$$
3x^2-3x+5-(3x^2+3)=-3x+2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
2x^3+3x^2-x+5
=
(x^2+1)(2x+3)+(-3x+2).
$$

Hence,

$$
\dfrac{2x^3+3x^2-x+5}{x^2+1}
=
2x+3+\dfrac{-3x+2}{x^2+1}.
$$

## 7.2 Partial fractions

### Key mathematical ideas

Partial fractions reverse the process of adding algebraic fractions.

This chapter uses three main proper-fraction types.

### Type 1: distinct linear factors

If the denominator has distinct linear factors, use one constant numerator over each factor.

For example,

$$
\dfrac{5x+1}{(x-1)(x+2)}
=
\dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{B}{x+2}.
$$

### Type 2: repeated linear factors

If the denominator has a repeated linear factor, include each power of that factor.

For example,

$$
\dfrac{3x+5}{(x-1)^2}
=
\dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{B}{(x-1)^2}.
$$

For a denominator such as $(x-1)^3$, we would use

$$
\dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{B}{(x-1)^2}+\dfrac{C}{(x-1)^3}.
$$

### Type 3: an irreducible quadratic factor

If the denominator contains a quadratic factor that cannot be factorised over the real numbers, use a linear numerator over that quadratic.

For example,

$$
\dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)}
=
\dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+2}.
$$

### Worked example

Express

$$
\dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)}
$$

in partial fractions.

Write

$$
\dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)}
=
\dfrac{A}{x-1}+\dfrac{Bx+C}{x^2+2}.
$$

Multiplying throughout by $(x-1)(x^2+2)$ gives

$$
7x-1=A(x^2+2)+(Bx+C)(x-1).
$$

Expanding,

$$
7x-1
=
A x^2+2A+Bx^2-Bx+Cx-C.
$$

Collecting terms,

$$
7x-1
=
(A+B)x^2+(C-B)x+(2A-C).
$$

Equating coefficients gives

$$
A+B=0,
$$

$$
C-B=7,
$$

and

$$
2A-C=-1.
$$

Solving gives

$$
A=2,\quad B=-2,\quad C=5.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{7x-1}{(x-1)(x^2+2)}
=
\dfrac{2}{x-1}+\dfrac{-2x+5}{x^2+2}.
$$

## 7.3 Binomial expansion of $(1+x)^n$ for values of $n$ that are not positive integers

### Key mathematical ideas

For any rational number $n$,

$$
(1+x)^n
=
1+nx+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2
+
\dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3
+\cdots
$$

provided

$$
|x|<1.
$$

When $n$ is not a non-negative integer, the expansion does not usually terminate. The more terms we include, the better the approximation tends to be inside the interval of validity.

## 7.4 Binomial expansion of $(a+x)^n$ for values of $n$ that are not positive integers

### Key mathematical ideas

To expand

$$
(a+x)^n,
$$

factor out $a$ first:

$$
(a+x)^n
=
a^n\left(1+\dfrac{x}{a}\right)^n.
$$

Then apply the binomial expansion to

$$
\left(1+\dfrac{x}{a}\right)^n.
$$

The expansion is valid when

$$
\left|\dfrac{x}{a}\right|<1.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
|x|<|a|.
$$

### Worked example

Expand

$$
(4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}}
$$

up to and including the term in $x^3$.

First write

$$
(4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}}
=
4^{\frac{1}{2}}\left(1+\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}.
$$

So

$$
(4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}}
=
2\left(1+\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}.
$$

Now

$$
(1+u)^{\frac{1}{2}}
=
1+\dfrac{1}{2}u-\dfrac{1}{8}u^2+\dfrac{1}{16}u^3+\cdots.
$$

Using

$$
u=\dfrac{x}{4},
$$

we get

$$
(4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}}
=
2\left[
1+\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{x}{4}\right)
-\dfrac{1}{8}\left(\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^2
+\dfrac{1}{16}\left(\dfrac{x}{4}\right)^3
+\cdots
\right].
$$

Therefore,

$$
(4+x)^{\frac{1}{2}}
=
2+\dfrac{x}{4}-\dfrac{x^2}{64}+\dfrac{x^3}{512}+\cdots.
$$

The expansion is valid when

$$
|x|<4.
$$

## 7.5 Partial fractions and binomial expansions

### Key mathematical ideas

Partial fractions are often used before binomial expansion.

For example,

$$
\dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)}
$$

can be split into simpler fractions:

$$
\dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)}
=
\dfrac{2}{1-x}-\dfrac{1}{1+2x}.
$$

Each term can then be expanded separately.

The expansion

$$
\dfrac{1}{1-x}
=
1+x+x^2+x^3+\cdots
$$

is valid for

$$
|x|<1.
$$

The expansion

$$
\dfrac{1}{1+2x}
=
1-2x+4x^2-8x^3+\cdots
$$

is valid for

$$
|2x|<1.
$$

So the combined expansion is valid for

$$
|x|<\dfrac{1}{2}.
$$

### Worked example

Obtain the expansion of

$$
\dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)}
$$

in ascending powers of $x$, up to and including the term in $x^3$.

First write

$$
\dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)}
=
\dfrac{A}{1-x}+\dfrac{B}{1+2x}.
$$

Multiplying through by $(1-x)(1+2x)$ gives

$$
1+5x=A(1+2x)+B(1-x).
$$

Equating coefficients gives

$$
A+B=1
$$

and

$$
2A-B=5.
$$

Solving gives

$$
A=2,\quad B=-1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)}
=
\dfrac{2}{1-x}-\dfrac{1}{1+2x}.
$$

Now,

$$
\dfrac{2}{1-x}
=
2(1+x+x^2+x^3+\cdots)
$$

and

$$
-\dfrac{1}{1+2x}
=
-(1-2x+4x^2-8x^3+\cdots).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\dfrac{1+5x}{(1-x)(1+2x)}
=
1+4x-2x^2+10x^3+\cdots.
$$

The expansion is valid when

$$
|x|<\dfrac{1}{2}.
$$

### Answers to STEP-style extension questions

1. The coefficient of $x^r$ is obtained by choosing the first $r$ descending factors from $n$ and dividing by $r!$:
   $$
   \dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-r+1)}{r!}.
   $$
   For $(1-2x)^{-\frac{1}{2}}$, the coefficient of $x^4$ is
   $$
   \dfrac{35}{128}\cdot16=\dfrac{35}{8}.
   $$

2. Since
   $$
   \dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}
   =
   \dfrac{1}{r}-\dfrac{1}{r+1},
   $$
   the sum telescopes:
   $$
   \sum_{r=1}^{n}\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}
   =
   \left(1-\dfrac{1}{2}\right)+\left(\dfrac{1}{2}-\dfrac{1}{3}\right)+\cdots+\left(\dfrac{1}{n}-\dfrac{1}{n+1}\right).
   $$
   Therefore,
   $$
   \sum_{r=1}^{n}\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}
   =
   1-\dfrac{1}{n+1}
   =
   \dfrac{n}{n+1}.
   $$

3. For $x\neq-1$,
   $$
   \dfrac{1+x}{(1-2x)(1+x)}
   =
   \dfrac{1}{1-2x}.
   $$
   If the expression is simplified first, the only binomial expansion needed is
   $$
   \dfrac{1}{1-2x}=1+2x+4x^2+\cdots,
   $$
   which is valid for $|x|<\dfrac{1}{2}$. Without simplification, the factor $1+x$ may misleadingly suggest another condition, but it cancels from the function.

4. Long division gives quotient $1$ and remainder $2x^2+4x$. Therefore,
   $$
   \dfrac{x^3+3x^2+5x+1}{(x+1)(x^2+1)}
   =
   1+\dfrac{2x^2+4x}{(x+1)(x^2+1)}.
   $$
   Now
   $$
   \dfrac{2x^2+4x}{(x+1)(x^2+1)}
   =
   -\dfrac{1}{x+1}+\dfrac{3x+1}{x^2+1}.
   $$
   Hence,
   $$
   \dfrac{x^3+3x^2+5x+1}{(x+1)(x^2+1)}
   =
   1-\dfrac{1}{x+1}+\dfrac{3x+1}{x^2+1}.
   $$

5. Expanding
   $$
   \dfrac{1+ax}{(1-x)(1+2x)}
   $$
   gives coefficient of $x^3$ equal to
   $$
   3a-5.
   $$
   Setting this equal to zero gives
   $$
   a=\dfrac{5}{3}.
   $$
